# MOD-D1-01: FERPA and Student Confidentiality

## In Plain Language

FERPA — the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act — is a federal law that controls who gets to see student educational records. Think of it this way: your student's test results, IEP, and behavior incident reports are legal documents, not casual information. A teacher sharing test results with a reading specialist down the hall, even with good intentions, is breaking FERPA. It does not matter if the specialist is qualified, well-meaning, or in the same building. What matters is whether the sharing was formally authorized. FERPA is the guardrail that keeps confidentiality boundaries clear, and school psychologists are the gatekeepers.

## First-Principles Explanation

FERPA exists because student educational records contain sensitive information that can shape how peers, teachers, and employers perceive a student. Federal law recognizes that parents have a right to control who sees their child's school records, and students have rights to their own records once they reach age 18. The law assumes that without clear rules, schools would casually share information in ways that harm students.

FERPA defines two categories of authorized access:

1. **School officials with a legitimate educational interest.** This means someone employed by the school (or an authorized contractor) who needs the information to do their job. A school psychologist evaluating a student has a legitimate educational interest. A teacher teaching that student has a legitimate educational interest. A teaching assistant assigned to support that student has a legitimate educational interest. But a teaching assistant with no assigned role in a student's case does not have automatic access.

2. **Parents and guardians.** Once a student turns 18 (or earlier if the student is emancipated), the student becomes the educational rights holder, and parents lose access unless the student is dependent on the parents' tax return.

Anyone else — a neighbor who is a reading specialist, a community counselor, a family friend who is a teacher — cannot access records without explicit parental consent in writing. "Directory information" (name, address, phone, enrollment dates) can be shared without consent unless a parent opts out.

The key insight: authorization is binary. Either access is permitted within a defined category, or it is not. There is no "gray area" where good intentions make a violation okay.

## Worked Example

A school psychologist, Ms. Chen, completes a comprehensive psychological evaluation for a 9-year-old student, James, referred for academic difficulties. The report includes cognitive test scores, recommendations for special education evaluation, and observations about James's anxiety.

Ms. Chen speaks to the general education teacher in the hallway and says, "I found that James has strong nonverbal reasoning but struggles with working memory. You might use manipulatives in math." The teacher nods appreciatively.

Later, Ms. Chen attends an IEP meeting with James's parents. She shares the full report, including detailed test scores and interpretations, and discusses the findings with the team.

The next day, the school librarian (not on the IEP team) asks Ms. Chen casually, "How is James doing?" Ms. Chen says, "I cannot share that without a parent's permission or a legitimate educational interest."

**Analysis:** The hallway conversation with the teacher was appropriate (teacher has a legitimate educational interest in James's learning needs). The IEP meeting with parents was appropriate (parents have rights to their child's records). The librarian's casual inquiry was appropriately declined (librarian has no assigned role in James's case). If the librarian were the school's reading specialist and James were assigned to her reading intervention, the answer would be different.

## Named Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "If the person is a professional, they can see the records."**
Reality: Professional credentials do not grant access. A licensed clinical psychologist who is not employed by the school cannot access records without parental consent, even though they are qualified. FERPA is about authorization, not competence.

**Misconception 2: "FERPA doesn't apply to behavior incident reports."**
Reality: FERPA applies to all educational records. An incident report about a student's behavior, a discipline suspension notice, and a teacher's behavior anecdotal notes are all educational records. They cannot be shared without authorization.

**Misconception 3: "We can share the information as long as we don't use the student's name."**
Reality: Identifying information other than name (student ID, age + school, address) also triggers FERPA. De-identifying does not bypass the rule; authorization does. Exception: data used for research or program evaluation can be shared in de-identified form per FERPA.

## If You Only Remember One Thing

**FERPA = Authorization, not expertise. If there is no formal authorization, do not share.**

## Concept-Specific Mini-Quiz Stems

1. **Stem:** A school counselor is not assigned to a specific student's case but overhears a conversation about the student's test results. Can the counselor request the full report?
   **Correct answer:** No; the counselor must have a legitimate educational interest (an assigned role in the student's case) to access records. Overhearing does not grant access.

2. **Stem:** Parents request a copy of their 17-year-old's IEP and psychological evaluation. The school says, "Your child is old enough to request their own records." Is this response correct?
   **Correct answer:** No. Under FERPA, parents have rights to their child's records until the student turns 18 (or is emancipated). Age 17 is still a parent's right.

3. **Stem:** A school psychologist discusses a student's evaluation results with the student's teacher in the hallway, sharing specific test scores and interpretations. The teacher has the student in class and will implement recommendations. Is this a FERPA violation?
   **Correct answer:** No. The teacher has a legitimate educational interest (the student is in the teacher's class), so the sharing is authorized. However, a private setting (not a hallway) is professionally better practice.
