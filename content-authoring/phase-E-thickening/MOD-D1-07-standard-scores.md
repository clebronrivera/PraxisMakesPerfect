# MOD-D1-07: Reading Standard Scores on Cognitive Assessments

## In Plain Language

On nearly all cognitive tests (WISC, WAIS, Woodcock-Johnson, Stanford-Binet), the average score is 100, and the standard deviation is 15 points. This scale matters because specific numbers mean specific things. A score of 100 is average. A score of 130 is very gifted (two standard deviations above average). A score of 61 puts a person firmly in the intellectual disability range — almost three standard deviations below average. The Praxis exam will give you a standard score and ask you to classify it (average, borderline, ID range, etc.). You need to know the benchmarks cold: 130+ is very superior, 115–129 is high average, 85–115 is average, 70–84 is borderline, 69 and below is ID range. These thresholds are not made up; they map to real percentiles and clinical implications.

## First-Principles Explanation

Standard scores (also called "scaled scores" in some contexts, though not identical) are the backbone of norm-referenced cognitive assessment. They allow comparison of a person's performance to a large, representative sample (the norm sample) that was tested when the instrument was developed.

The standard score scale works like this:

- **Mean = 100**
- **Standard Deviation = 15 points** (on most modern tests; some older tests use SD = 16)
- **One SD = 15 points**

This creates easy-to-remember benchmarks:
- 100 is the mean.
- 115 (100 + 15) is one SD above: high average.
- 130 (100 + 30) is two SDs above: very superior.
- 85 (100 − 15) is one SD below: low average.
- 70 (100 − 30) is two SDs below: borderline.
- 55 (100 − 45) is three SDs below: intellectual disability range.

**Percentile equivalents:**
- SS 100 = 50th percentile (right in the middle)
- SS 115 = 84th percentile (higher than 84% of the norm sample)
- SS 130 = 98th percentile (higher than 98% of the norm sample)
- SS 85 = 16th percentile (lower than 84% of the norm sample, or put another way, equal to or higher than 16%)
- SS 70 = 2nd percentile (very low)
- SS 61 = 0.5th percentile (extremely low)

**Clinical classifications** based on standard score ranges:

| Range | Clinical Label | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| 130+ | Very Superior / Gifted | Exceptional cognitive ability; consider advanced placement |
| 115–129 | High Average / Above Average | Above the norm; strong learning capacity |
| 100–114 | Average | Within normal range; typical performance |
| 85–99 | Low Average | Below average but not a disorder; within normal limits |
| 70–84 | Borderline | Significant cognitive limitations; below average; potential support need |
| Below 70 | Intellectual Disability Range | Significant cognitive limitation that, combined with adaptive functioning deficits, may meet criteria for Intellectual Disability |

**Key thresholds:**

1. **SS 70** is the classic threshold. Scores at or below 70 are considered ID range. This is approximately 2 standard deviations below the mean and represents about 2–3% of the population. For an IDEA determination of Intellectual Disability, the student must score at or below 70 on a comprehensive cognitive battery (IQ test) AND show deficits in adaptive functioning AND have evidence of a developmental origin.

2. **SS 85** is the borderline-to-low-average divide. A score of 85 is at the 16th percentile — low compared to the average student, but still within the typical range. This student might struggle with academic demands but does not meet criteria for ID.

3. **SS 69** is technically one point above 70, but it is still ID range. A score of 61, 65, or 69 is all ID range; scores above 70 are borderline.

**Why this matters on the exam:**

Questions will present a standard score and ask you to classify it. Example: "A student's cognitive composite is 61. This is best described as..."

- (A) Low average — NO. 61 is ID range, not low average. Low average is 80–89.
- (B) Borderline — NO. Borderline is 70–84. 61 is below borderline, into ID range.
- (C) Intellectual disability range — YES. 61 is more than two SDs below 100.

You must also recognize that standard scores are continuous and context-dependent. A score of 85 in one domain (say, verbal reasoning) and 115 in another (say, nonverbal reasoning) tells a story about uneven cognitive development. The overall IQ is the average of the subtests, and the profile of highs and lows is clinically important.

## Worked Example

Three students take the WISC-5 (Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children, Fifth Edition):

**Student A — Amara:**
Full Scale IQ = 105 (average). All subtests in the 95–115 range. **Interpretation:** Amara has typically developing cognitive abilities. No concern for ID. No evidence of a specific cognitive strength or weakness. She is performing as expected for her age.

**Student B — Benito:**
Full Scale IQ = 72. Verbal Comprehension = 78 (borderline). Perceptual Reasoning = 68 (ID range). Working Memory = 72 (borderline). Processing Speed = 75 (borderline). **Interpretation:** Benito's cognitive abilities are globally low, consistent with the ID range. All domains are impaired. Combined with adaptive functioning assessment (which the psychologist will do with the Vineland), Benito may meet criteria for Intellectual Disability. A score of 72 is clearly ID range, not borderline.

**Student C — Cara:**
Full Scale IQ = 94 (average overall, but uneven profile). Verbal Comprehension = 110 (high average). Perceptual Reasoning = 88 (low average). Working Memory = 82 (low average). Processing Speed = 85 (low average). **Interpretation:** Cara's verbal skills are a relative strength, but her reasoning and processing speed are weaker. The overall IQ of 94 is average, but the profile suggests she may have a specific learning disability in a non-verbal or processing speed domain. She does NOT have intellectual disability (the overall IQ is average and there is a clear strength area), but she may have a specific learning disability requiring targeted support.

**Scenario:** A question asks: "Benito's WISC-5 Full Scale IQ is 72. What does this indicate?"

- (A) Average cognitive ability — NO. 72 is below 85, so it is not average.
- (B) Below average cognitive ability — Ambiguous. Technically true, but misses the clinical significance.
- (C) Intellectual disability range — YES. 72 is two standard deviations below 100, in the ID range.

The correct answer is (C). It is specific and clinically accurate.

## Named Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "SS 69 and SS 70 are different; one is ID and one is borderline."**
Reality: Both 69 and 70 are in the ID range. SS 70 is the classic threshold, but 69 is also ID range (less than 2 SDs below mean). The distinction between borderline and ID is at SS 70; anything 70 and below is ID range, anything 71–84 is borderline.

**Misconception 2: "A score of 85 means the student is borderline and may have ID."**
Reality: 85 is low-average, not borderline. Borderline is 70–84. A score of 85 is at the 16th percentile — low compared to peers, but within typical range and does not suggest ID.

**Misconception 3: "If a student scores 120 on one subtest and 70 on another, the student's overall IQ is the average of the two (95), so the student is average."**
Reality: This is mathematically correct but clinically wrong. A student with a 120/70 split is likely to have a significant specific learning disability or cognitive profile disorder. The profile matters more than the average when there is extreme scatter.

**Misconception 4: "Standard deviation is always 15 points."**
Reality: For most modern cognitive tests (WISC, WAIS, Woodcock-Johnson, BASC behavioral scales), it is 15. But some older tests use 16. When reading test manuals, always check the stated mean and SD.

**Misconception 5: "SS 85 is borderline."**
Reality: 85 is low-average. Borderline is 70–84. This is a common and important distinction on the Praxis exam.

## If You Only Remember One Thing

**SS 100 is average. Every 15 points is one standard deviation. Two SDs below 100 = 70 = the ID threshold. Know these numbers cold.**

## Mnemonic

**"100 + 30 = 130 (very superior), 100 − 30 = 70 (ID threshold). Everything in between fits the scale."**

Or: **"100-115-130" (average-high-very superior), **"100-85-70" (average-low-ID).****

## Concept-Specific Mini-Quiz Stems

1. **Stem:** A student's cognitive assessment yields a Full Scale IQ of 68. How should this score be classified?
   **Correct answer:** Intellectual disability range. A score of 68 is more than two standard deviations below the mean of 100 and falls below the SS 70 threshold.

2. **Stem:** A student scores 82 on a cognitive battery. The teacher says, "That is borderline; the student probably has intellectual disability." Is this correct?
   **Correct answer:** No. A score of 82 is low-average (borderline is 70–84, but 82 is on the higher end of that range, approaching low-average). More importantly, ID is not determined by IQ alone; adaptive functioning must also be impaired. An IQ of 82 alone does not support an ID determination.

3. **Stem:** Two students both score in the average range on overall IQ (103 and 101), but one student has a Verbal Comprehension score of 115 and a Perceptual Reasoning score of 88, while the other has all subtests clustered between 100 and 105. Which profile suggests a possible specific learning disability?
   **Correct answer:** The first student. The large scatter (115 vs. 88 = 27-point gap) between strength (Verbal) and weakness (Perceptual Reasoning) suggests uneven cognitive development consistent with a specific learning disability in nonverbal reasoning or visual-spatial processing.
