# MOD-D1-08: Reliability and Validity — What the Numbers Mean

## In Plain Language

Every psychological test has two fundamental quality measures: reliability (does it give consistent results?) and validity (does it actually measure what it says it measures?). Reliability is like a dartboard where you throw five darts — a reliable board has all your darts in a tight cluster, even if they are not in the bullseye. Validity is hitting the bullseye itself. A test can be reliable without being valid (consistent but wrong), but it cannot be valid without being reliable (you cannot hit the target if your throws are wildly scattered). On the Praxis exam, you will see coefficients (numbers like 0.79 or 0.58) and have to interpret them. Reliability and validity coefficients above 0.80 are generally preferred, though standards differ by context. A coefficient of 0.79 is acceptable but marginal. A coefficient of 0.58 indicates only moderate agreement and is not strong enough to endorse a new test as a replacement for an established one.

## First-Principles Explanation

**Reliability** refers to consistency and reproducibility. It answers: "If I test the same person under the same conditions on different occasions, do I get the same score?" Reliable tests are stable, internally consistent, and free from random error.

Types of reliability coefficients:

1. **Test-Retest Reliability.** The same test is given to the same person at two different times (e.g., 2 weeks apart). Scores should be similar. A correlation of 0.85 is good; 0.79 is acceptable but marginal; below 0.75 raises concerns about stability.

2. **Internal Consistency.** A test's items correlate with each other (they measure the same construct). Measured with Cronbach's Alpha or similar. Ideally 0.80 or higher. An alpha of 0.65 suggests the items are not tightly related (construct is not coherent).

3. **Inter-Rater Reliability.** When multiple people score a test (common with projectives like the Rorschach), do they agree? Measured with correlation or kappa statistic. Ideally 0.80 or higher for clinical purposes.

Standard error of measurement (SEM) is another way to express reliability: the average error you expect around a person's obtained score. If a test's SEM is 5 points and a student scores 100, their true score is approximately 95–105 (100 ± 5).

**Validity** refers to whether the test measures what it claims to measure. A test can be reliable but invalid. Example: a scale that reliably measures height is not valid for measuring intelligence, even though it is reliable.

Types of validity coefficients:

1. **Convergent Validity.** Does the new test correlate with other established tests of the same construct? If a new intelligence test correlates 0.90 with the WISC, convergent validity is strong. A correlation of 0.58 indicates only moderate association and is not strong evidence that the new test measures the same thing.

2. **Discriminant Validity.** Does the test NOT correlate with measures of different constructs? An intelligence test should correlate weakly with a personality test. A correlation of 0.10–0.20 is good; 0.50+ suggests the test is measuring something it should not.

3. **Construct Validity.** Does the test behave the way theory predicts? If working memory theory predicts that WM scores should improve with age during childhood (up to early adulthood) then plateau, do the test scores show this pattern?

4. **Criterion Validity.** Does the test predict real-world outcomes? Does a reading assessment at kindergarten predict reading success in first grade? Does a depression screener identify students who later require mental health services?

The practical standard: **Coefficients of 0.80 or above are preferred for both reliability and validity.** Coefficients of 0.70–0.79 are acceptable but marginal. Below 0.70, the test's adequacy is questionable without additional context.

## Worked Example

A new reading comprehension test is developed and validated. The researchers report:

**Reliability Results:**
- Test-retest (2-week interval): r = 0.87 (good; scores are stable over time)
- Internal consistency (Cronbach's Alpha): α = 0.82 (good; items hang together)
- **Overall Reliability Assessment: ADEQUATE**

**Validity Results:**
- Convergent validity with WISC Reading Comprehension subtest: r = 0.78 (moderate to good; the new test correlates with an established measure, but the correlation is not extremely high)
- Convergent validity with Gates-MacGinitie Reading Comprehension: r = 0.72 (moderate; decent association with another established test)
- Discriminant validity with the BASC Depression subscale: r = 0.15 (good; the reading test does not correlate with depression, as expected)
- Criterion validity: Students who scored above-average on the new test in Grade 3 had 82% probability of meeting grade-level reading standards in Grade 4. (good predictive power)
- **Overall Validity Assessment: ADEQUATE but with caveat**

**Interpretation:** The new test has acceptable reliability. Its validity is mixed. The convergent validity correlations (0.72–0.78) are moderate. They suggest the new test is measuring something related to reading comprehension, but the correlations are not strong enough to say the new test is equivalent to the WISC or Gates-MacGinitie. If a school is considering replacing the WISC Reading Comprehension subtest with this new test, a correlation of 0.78 is borderline. A validity coefficient of 0.85+ would be more convincing.

However, the criterion validity (predicting Grade 4 success) is strong, suggesting the test does have practical value for predicting future reading performance, even if it is not identical to other tests.

**Research Recommendation:** "The new test is acceptable for supplementary assessment and may be useful as a screener. We do not recommend replacing established measures like the WISC RCS without further validation studies showing stronger convergent validity (0.85+)."

**Exam Question Example:** "A new behavioral assessment shows test-retest reliability of 0.79 and convergent validity with an established measure of 0.58. Which statement is most accurate?

(A) The test is both reliable and valid; recommended for use. — NO. 0.79 reliability is marginal, and 0.58 validity is only moderate.

(B) The test is reliable but shows only moderate validity; should not be adopted as a replacement for established measures. — YES. 0.79 is acceptable for reliability but at the lower end. 0.58 validity is moderate and not strong enough to endorse it as a primary measure.

(C) The test has poor reliability and should not be used. — NO. 0.79 is acceptable, not poor.

(D) Reliability is irrelevant if validity is established. — NO. Both matter. Low reliability undermines validity.

**Correct answer: (B)**

## Named Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "A reliable test must be valid."**
Reality: No. A test can be highly reliable and completely invalid. A ruler is a reliable instrument for measuring length, but it is not valid for measuring intelligence. Reliability is necessary but not sufficient for validity.

**Misconception 2: "A validity coefficient of 0.65 is good enough for adoption."**
Reality: Context matters, but 0.65 is generally considered low-to-moderate. For clinical or high-stakes decisions, 0.70+ is preferred, and 0.80+ is the gold standard. A coefficient of 0.65 suggests the test shares only about 42% of its variance with the criterion measure (r² = 0.65² = 0.42).

**Misconception 3: "Validity is the only thing that matters; reliability is secondary."**
Reality: Both matter. A test with poor reliability has random error that undermines its validity. Even if a test is theoretically valid, random measurement error makes it unreliable and thus less useful in practice.

**Misconception 4: "A test-retest reliability of 0.79 is strong."**
Reality: 0.79 is acceptable but marginal. The convention is 0.80 or higher for good test-retest reliability. A coefficient of 0.79 means only about 62% of the variance is shared between the two administrations (r² = 0.79²). That is acceptable, but practitioners prefer higher stability.

**Misconception 5: "If a test is used in schools, it must be reliable and valid."**
Reality: Many tests in use fall short of ideal psychometric standards. A test being widely used does not guarantee it has strong reliability and validity coefficients. Always check the manual.

## If You Only Remember One Thing

**Reliability = consistency. Validity = accuracy. You need both. Coefficients of 0.80+ are preferred. Below 0.70 is questionable. A coefficient of 0.79 is marginal; 0.58 is moderate.**

## Analogy

**Reliability is a dartboard with tight clustering. Validity is hitting the bullseye.** You can have tight clustering without hitting the target (reliable but not valid). You cannot hit the target if your darts are scattered everywhere (low reliability = impossible high validity).

## Concept-Specific Mini-Quiz Stems

1. **Stem:** A new reading assessment shows test-retest reliability of 0.81 and convergent validity with the WISC-5 Reading Subtest of 0.72. How should practitioners interpret this?
   **Correct answer:** The test has acceptable reliability and moderate validity. It is consistent in measurement, but the moderate convergent validity (0.72) suggests it is not measuring exactly the same construct as the WISC-5. It could be used to supplement the WISC-5 but not replace it.

2. **Stem:** A school psychologist is choosing between two new behavioral rating scales. Test A has reliability of 0.85 and validity of 0.75. Test B has reliability of 0.78 and validity of 0.82. Which test should be recommended?
   **Correct answer:** Depends on priority, but Test A is probably safer (both coefficients are in acceptable range, with reliability particularly strong). Test B has strong validity but marginal reliability; marginal reliability is a larger concern because low reliability undermines validity. (However, a case could be made for Test B if criterion validity prediction is the primary goal.)

3. **Stem:** A test manual reports Cronbach's Alpha = 0.68 for internal consistency. What does this suggest?
   **Correct answer:** Internal consistency is below the preferred threshold of 0.70–0.80. The test's items may not be tightly related, suggesting the construct being measured may not be coherent. This test may need revision or supplementary measures to ensure construct validity.
