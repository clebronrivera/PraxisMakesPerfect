# MOD-D1-02: RTI Data — Universal Screening vs. Progress Monitoring

## In Plain Language

Response to Intervention (RTI) is a system where schools use data to catch students who are falling behind and give them extra help. There are two types of data collection that seem similar but serve completely different jobs. Universal screening is a quick, low-cost test everyone takes at the same time — like a blood pressure check at a health fair — to find out who might need more help. Progress monitoring is checking up repeatedly on a student who is already getting extra help to see if it is working. Getting these two straight is critical on the Praxis exam because the test loves to use fake-sounding names for them.

## First-Principles Explanation

RTI is fundamentally a system of measurement at different scales. The system asks: "How many students are at risk?" (universal screening) and then "Is my intervention actually helping this student?" (progress monitoring). These are different questions requiring different tools and frequencies.

**Universal Screening** is the epidemiological layer. Like a school nurse doing height and weight checks on all students, universal screening is brief (5–10 minutes), low-cost, and administered to everyone in the relevant grade or school. The goal is to identify students who fall below a criterion benchmark — perhaps the 25th percentile in reading fluency or the bottom quartile in mathematics computation. Universal screening is typically done 2–3 times per year (fall, winter, spring) to catch emerging risk before problems compound. It is the front door to the RTI system.

**Progress Monitoring** is the intensive follow-up layer. Once a student has been identified as at-risk and placed in an intervention, progress monitoring measures that specific student's progress on that specific skill, frequently — often weekly or biweekly. The goal is to determine whether the intervention is actually working for this particular student. If a student is not making progress after 4–6 weeks of intervention, the team adjusts the intervention or changes the approach.

The difference in frequency and purpose is crucial. A teacher cannot use progress monitoring data to replace universal screening. You cannot screen a whole school by monitoring one student's progress. Conversely, universal screening data alone cannot tell you whether an intervention is effective; you need the repeated, frequent measures of progress monitoring.

## Worked Example

Lincoln Elementary uses RTI in reading. In September, all students in grades K–3 take a 2-minute oral reading fluency (ORF) probe from their grade-level curriculum. This is universal screening. The data shows that 18% of second graders fall below the 25th percentile for fluency. These 18% are flagged as at-risk.

In October, those 18 second graders begin a 4-week small-group reading intervention (Tier 2) with a reading specialist. Every week, each student reads a brief ORF probe (same length, same format) and the specialist records the fluency score. This is progress monitoring. After 4 weeks, the data shows:
- Group A (8 students): Fluency improved 15–22 words per minute. The intervention is working; continue it.
- Group B (10 students): Fluency improved only 2–5 words per minute. Intervention effectiveness is questionable. The team adjusts to more intensive (Tier 3) intervention or changes the instructional approach.

In December, all students take the universal screening again to check the broader picture and catch any new students entering the at-risk zone.

**Analysis:** Universal screening identified the initial group. Progress monitoring tracked the intervention's effectiveness. The distinction shaped the decision (continue vs. adjust) and the intervention path (Tier 2 vs. escalation to Tier 3).

## Named Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Universal Monitoring" and "Progress Screening" are real RTI terms.**
Reality: They sound plausible but are not valid terms. Exam writers use these fake terms as distractors. If you see "Universal Monitoring" or "Progress Screening" in an answer choice, eliminate it immediately.

**Misconception 2: "Progress monitoring can replace universal screening; we just monitor the whole class."**
Reality: Universal screening is for identifying risk across a population. Progress monitoring is for tracking one student's response to one intervention. Monitoring one student's weekly progress does not identify other at-risk students in the grade.

**Misconception 3: "Progress monitoring must be done monthly."**
Reality: Progress monitoring typically occurs weekly or biweekly, not monthly. Monthly is too infrequent to adjust interventions quickly if they are not working.

**Misconception 4: "Universal screening should happen weekly."**
Reality: Universal screening happens 2–3 times per year (fall, winter, spring). Weekly would be resource-intensive and unnecessary for identifying broad risk. Progress monitoring is the frequent measure.

## If You Only Remember One Thing

**Screening finds who is at-risk (a few times per year); monitoring tracks whether help is working (weekly or biweekly).**

## Analogy

**Screening is like a thermometer you use to check the whole family in a health crisis.** Progress monitoring is like the daily temperature checks on the one person who is sick to see if the medicine is working.

## Concept-Specific Mini-Quiz Stems

1. **Stem:** A school psychologist administers a 5-minute math computation screener to all third graders in September. This is an example of:
   **Correct answer:** Universal screening. It is brief, low-cost, and administered to the whole grade to identify at-risk students.

2. **Stem:** A student identified as at-risk in reading begins a small-group intervention. The teacher measures the student's reading fluency every Tuesday for 8 weeks. This is an example of:
   **Correct answer:** Progress monitoring. It measures a specific student's response to a specific intervention over time.

3. **Stem:** A teacher wants to know which of 5 intervention approaches is most effective for a student on a Tier 2 reading intervention. What data would best answer this question?
   **Correct answer:** Progress monitoring data with the student comparing fluency scores across different intervention weeks or phases. (If the teacher switches methods mid-intervention, progress monitoring shows which method yielded better gains.)
