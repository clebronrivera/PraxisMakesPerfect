# MOD-D1-03: Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA) — Required First Step

## In Plain Language

Before a school psychologist or IEP team writes any formal behavior support plan, there must be a Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA). An FBA is a detective process: you watch a student's behavior closely, figure out what triggers it (what happens before), what the student actually does (the behavior itself), and what happens afterward that might be keeping the behavior going. This is called the ABC model — Antecedent, Behavior, Consequence. Without understanding why the behavior is happening and what is rewarding it, any plan is a guess. The Praxis exam will ask you to identify FBA as the legally and clinically required first step before writing a Behavior Intervention Plan.

## First-Principles Explanation

IDEA (the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act) and best-practice standards require that before a school implements an individualized behavior plan, the team must understand the function of the behavior. Behavior does not happen in a vacuum. All behavior is communication and all behavior is maintained by consequences.

The FBA process rests on three pillars:

1. **Antecedent (what comes before).** This is the trigger. Does the behavior occur at specific times (transitions, unstructured time, math class)? With specific people (substitutes, certain peers)? During certain tasks (independent work, group projects)? The antecedent is the environmental condition that sets the stage.

2. **Behavior (what the student does).** This must be specific and observable. "Defiant" is not a behavior; "yelling, refusing to follow directions, walking away" is. Specificity matters because you cannot intervene on something vague.

3. **Consequence (what follows and maintains it).** This is the critical piece. Does the behavior get the student out of a task they do not want to do (escape function)? Does it get adult attention (attention function)? Does it result in peer admiration (peer reinforcement function)? Consequences are reinforcing if the behavior continues.

The FBA generates a hypothesis: "When [antecedent], the student exhibits [behavior] in order to [function]." For example: "When given independent math work, Maria refuses to start in order to escape the task because she lacks computational fluency." Once you have that hypothesis, interventions can target the root cause (in Maria's case: skill building and modified task demands, not punishment).

An FBA can be simple (brief observations + structured interview) or complex (extended observation, rating scales, testing). For a first evaluation in a standard case, a brief FBA is appropriate. For a student with severe or self-injurious behavior, a more intensive FBA by a Board Certified Behavior Analyst (BCBA) may be warranted.

## Worked Example

Miguel, a sixth grader, is referred to the school psychologist for chronic non-compliance. The teacher reports, "He refuses to do his work. He talks back, gets up from his seat without permission, and argues."

The school psychologist observes Miguel in math class (20 minutes) and in lunch (20 minutes). Notes:
- **In math:** Miguel starts his worksheet, writes one answer, then puts his head down. When the teacher approaches to check his work, Miguel says "I do not get it" and refuses to continue. The teacher leaves him alone. Two minutes later, Miguel whispers to a neighbor.
- **In lunch:** Miguel socializes, eats, takes appropriate turns in conversation. No refusal behavior observed.

**FBA Hypothesis:** The behavior is more likely to occur in academic settings than unstructured settings. It appears to be triggered by task difficulty (inability to solve the problem). The consequence that maintains it is escape from the task — when Miguel refuses, the teacher backs off.

The psychologist interviews the teacher and Miguel. Miguel says, "I don't know how to do the problems. It's easier to get in trouble than to ask for help."

**FBA Function:** Escape from a difficult task, possibly compounded by shame about asking for help.

**Resulting Behavior Intervention Plan:**
- **Antecedent modification:** Provide easier problems first, or problems similar to examples recently completed.
- **Teaching new skill:** Explicitly teach Miguel how to ask for help ("I need an example" or a hand signal).
- **Consequence:** When Miguel attempts the problem, provide immediate positive feedback. When he asks for help, provide it enthusiastically. When he refuses, do NOT continue the task — move to a different subject and come back later.

Without the FBA, the team might have focused on "compliance training" or punishment (detention, loss of recess). With the FBA, the intervention targets the actual function (escape due to skill gap) and teaches a more appropriate way to get help.

## Named Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "A social-emotional assessment can replace an FBA."**
Reality: A social-emotional assessment (like a SEL screener or emotional regulation scale) measures constructs like empathy or self-control. An FBA measures the specific antecedent-behavior-consequence chain for a particular behavior in a particular student. They serve different purposes and both may be useful, but only the FBA identifies function.

**Misconception 2: "Behavior plans should be written first, then an FBA can justify them."**
Reality: The FBA must come first, logically and legally. The FBA findings drive the behavior plan, not the other way around. If a team skips the FBA and jumps to a plan, the plan is not grounded in evidence about function.

**Misconception 3: "FBA is only needed for severe behaviors."**
Reality: An FBA is required under IDEA for any behavior that impedes learning. It does not have to be extensive, but it is required before a formal behavior plan.

**Misconception 4: "If we know the behavior is intentional, we understand the function."**
Reality: All behavior is intentional in the sense that it serves a purpose. But the specific function (escape, attention, sensory, power) determines the intervention. Knowing "he does it on purpose" does not tell you the function.

## If You Only Remember One Thing

**Before a Behavior Intervention Plan, always: FBA. FBA identifies function. Function drives intervention.**

## Analogy

**An FBA is like a medical diagnostic workup.** A doctor does not prescribe medication without understanding the diagnosis. A school psychologist does not prescribe a behavior plan without understanding the function. The function is the diagnosis.

## Concept-Specific Mini-Quiz Stems

1. **Stem:** A student is referred for excessive hand-raising and calling out in class. Before writing a behavior plan, what should the school psychologist do first?
   **Correct answer:** Conduct an FBA to understand the antecedent (when does it occur?), the specific behavior (what does the student do?), and the function (is the student seeking attention, trying to demonstrate knowledge, avoiding another task?).

2. **Stem:** An FBA reveals that a student's disruptive behavior occurs during independent work, the behavior is refusing to start, and the consequence is that the teacher moves the student to a different task. What is the most likely function of this behavior?
   **Correct answer:** Escape from the difficult task. The student avoids the task by refusing, and this is reinforced by the teacher providing an alternative.

3. **Stem:** Two students both refuse to complete assignments. Student A refuses when given novel, difficult tasks. Student B refuses right before lunch. These students require different behavior plans because:
   **Correct answer:** The FBA would reveal different functions — Student A's behavior is likely task-avoidance due to skill gaps; Student B's behavior might be attention-seeking or peer-influenced (timing near a preferred activity). The function determines the intervention.
