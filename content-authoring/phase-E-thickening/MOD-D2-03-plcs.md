# MOD-D2-03: Professional Learning Communities (PLCs) — The Three Driving Questions

## In Plain Language

A Professional Learning Community (PLC) is a team of teachers (and sometimes other staff) who meet regularly to look at student data and improve teaching. PLCs are driven by three specific, focused questions that keep everyone on task. Question 1: "What do we want students to learn?" (Define the goal.) Question 2: "How will we know when students have learned it?" (Measure success.) Question 3: "How will we respond when a student is struggling?" (Intervene.) The genius of PLCs is that these three questions are simple, powerful, and force continuous improvement. A school psychologist often facilitates or participates in PLCs to bring assessment expertise and help interpret data. On the Praxis exam, you will be asked to identify the three driving questions or explain what PLCs are NOT (e.g., it is not about how to present data prettily, or what tools to use to collect data — those are logistics, not the core questions).

## First-Principles Explanation

PLCs emerged from the work of Rick DuFour and Robert Eaker in the late 1990s and have become a dominant model for school improvement and collaborative professional development. The PLC model is built on four beliefs:

1. **Shared vision and mission:** The school community is united around clear goals for student learning.
2. **Collaborative culture:** Teachers work together, sharing responsibility for all students' learning.
3. **Focus on results:** Data on student learning drives decisions, not tradition or assumption.
4. **Continuous improvement:** The team constantly examines practice and adjusts.

**The three driving questions:**

These are not negotiable or optional. They form the skeleton of every PLC meeting:

1. **"What do we want each student to learn?"**
   - This requires the team to define learning standards and goals explicitly
   - Not vague ("students will be better readers") but specific ("students will decode CVC words with 90% accuracy by end of first quarter")
   - Based on grade-level standards, district curriculum, and sometimes individual student needs
   - Creates clarity about what success looks like

2. **"How will we know when each student has learned it?"**
   - This requires the team to define how they will measure learning
   - Could include formative assessments (daily checks, quick quizzes), summative assessments (unit tests), or performance tasks
   - The key point: measurement is intentional and aligned to the learning goal
   - Not about how to present or display data; about defining what counts as evidence of learning

3. **"How will we respond when a student experiences difficulty in learning?"**
   - This is the intervention and support question
   - What will we do for students who are not learning? (Reteach, small group intervention, different strategy, extra practice)
   - What will we do for students who are learning faster than peers? (Enrichment, extension, challenge)
   - Creates a system of support and challenge, not a one-size-fits-all approach
   - Often called "response to intervention" in a PLC context

**What PLCs are NOT:**

This is important for exam questions that try to trick you. PLCs are not:

- **Data dashboards or presentation formats.** PLCs might use charts and graphs, but the tool is secondary. The questions are primary.
- **Prescriptive about assessment methods.** The PLC does not mandate a specific test or measurement tool; the team decides based on their goals and context.
- **About compliance or accountability.** While PLCs often improve test scores, that is not the driving goal; the goal is authentic student learning.
- **Limited to one subject or grade.** PLCs can be grade-level teams, subject-area teams, whole-school teams, or even cross-school networks.
- **Quick fixes.** PLCs are ongoing, cyclical. A single meeting is not a PLC; it is a process over time.

**The role of the school psychologist in PLCs:**

School psychologists often support PLCs by:
- Helping the team interpret data (especially behavioral, social-emotional, or special education data)
- Providing professional development on assessment literacy
- Facilitating discussions about at-risk students and intervention options
- Connecting PLC work to RTI, MTSS, and special education processes
- Ensuring that measurement is valid and reliable

## Worked Example

**PLC Scenario — Elementary Reading**

The second-grade team meets as a PLC. Members include three second-grade teachers, the reading specialist, the special educator, and the school psychologist.

**Question 1: "What do we want students to learn?"**

The team reviews the grade-level reading standards and decides that by the end of second grade, students should be able to "read grade-level passages with 95%+ accuracy and answer comprehension questions with 80%+ accuracy."

They break this into quarterly goals:
- Q1: Decode CVC and simple two-syllable words with 90%+ accuracy
- Q2: Decode words with common digraphs, blends, and short vowel patterns with 90%+ accuracy
- Q3: Read grade-level passages with 85%+ accuracy and answer factual comprehension questions
- Q4: Read grade-level passages with 95%+ accuracy and answer both factual and inference comprehension questions

**Question 2: "How will we know when students have learned it?"**

The team decides on measurement:
- Weekly curriculum-based measurement (CBM) reading fluency probes from their core reading series (words correct per minute)
- Every other week, a brief comprehension check (5 multiple choice questions on a short passage)
- Mid-quarter and end-of-quarter unit assessments (provided by the reading series)
- Progress monitoring for students receiving intervention (weekly)

**Question 3: "How will we respond when a student experiences difficulty in learning?"**

The team develops a response plan:
- Students scoring below the Q1 benchmark in October: Small-group reteaching of phonics 3x per week, focusing on their specific gaps (e.g., one group works on CVC, another on initial blends)
- Students scoring significantly above benchmark: Enrichment with more complex texts, guided by the reading specialist
- Students with persistent gaps despite Tier 2 intervention: Referral for evaluation and possible special education services

**Ongoing PLC Work:**

Every week, the team:
1. Looks at the week's CBM data
2. Identifies students who are below benchmark
3. Adjusts small-group placements or intervention focus
4. Celebrates students hitting milestones

Every month:
1. Reviews comprehension data
2. Discusses which instructional strategies are working
3. Adjusts core instruction if needed

**School psychologist's role:** Helps interpret the data, especially for students with suspected learning disabilities. Facilitates discussion about whether students need special education evaluation or Tier 2 intervention. Ensures measurement is valid (Are the probes reliable? Do the scores predict end-of-year performance?).

**Result:** By the end of the school year, the team has a data-driven picture of each student's progress, they have responded promptly to students who struggled, and they have documentation to inform intervention or special education referrals. The cycle is ready to repeat next year with Q1 data and refined goals.

**Example: What this PLC is NOT:**

- It is not primarily about creating a pretty data dashboard (though they might display data).
- It is not about mandating a specific assessment tool (they chose CBM, but another team might use DIBELS or iReady).
- It is not about compliance with standardized tests (though the goals align with them).
- It is not a one-time meeting; it is ongoing and cyclical.

## Named Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "The three PLC questions are about data collection and presentation."**
Reality: The three questions are about learning goals, measurement definition, and intervention response. Data collection is a tool to answer these questions, not the questions themselves.

**Misconception 2: "PLCs must use a specific assessment tool or data platform."**
Reality: PLCs are agnostic about tools. The key is that measurement is intentional and aligned to goals. Different PLCs will use different tools based on their context.

**Misconception 3: "If we have one PLC meeting per quarter, we are a PLC."**
Reality: PLCs are ongoing, cyclical processes. Meetings should happen regularly (weekly or biweekly) with consistent focus on the three questions.

**Misconception 4: "The first question is about data. What data do we want?"**
Reality: The first question is "What do we want students to LEARN?" not "What data do we want to collect?" The data comes later (Question 2); it supports the learning goal.

**Misconception 5: "PLCs are mainly for elementary schools and reading/math."**
Reality: PLCs work at any level (elementary, middle, high) and in any subject (science, social studies, arts) and for any domain (academic, behavioral, social-emotional).

## If You Only Remember One Thing

**The three PLC questions: What do we want them to learn? How will we know? How will we respond when they struggle? These are the engine of continuous improvement.**

## Analogy

**A PLC is like a team of doctors reviewing patient outcomes and adjusting treatment.** What is the health goal? How do we measure it? How do we treat patients who are not getting better? Continuous improvement based on data.

## Concept-Specific Mini-Quiz Stems

1. **Stem:** A second-grade PLC is meeting to discuss reading. The agenda includes reviewing data on students' reading fluency (measured by CBM weekly), identifying students below benchmark, and planning small-group reteaching for those students. Which of the three PLC driving questions is being addressed?
   **Correct answer:** Question 3: "How will we respond when a student experiences difficulty in learning?" The team is using data (from Question 2's measurement) to inform intervention response.

2. **Stem:** A PLC spends most of its meeting time deciding which data dashboard tool to use to display student results. Is this an appropriate use of PLC time?
   **Correct answer:** No. The tool is secondary; the focus should be on the three driving questions. Time might be spent discussing how to measure learning (Question 2) or reviewing results, but the primary focus should be on goals, measurement, and response — not the tool itself.

3. **Stem:** A high school PLC defines their learning goal as "Students will understand photosynthesis." At the next meeting, they plan a PLC discussion. What should they prioritize?
   **Correct answer:** Question 2: How will we know students understand photosynthesis? (How will we measure it?) A more specific goal would also help (Question 1: What specific aspects of photosynthesis and what level of understanding?). But the sequence is typically Goal → Measurement → Response.
