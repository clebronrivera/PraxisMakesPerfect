# MOD-D2-02: Consultee-Centered Consultation — Building the Teacher, Not Solving the Problem

## In Plain Language

In consultee-centered consultation, the goal shifts from "let us fix this one student's problem" to "let us develop your skills so you can fix similar problems yourself in the future." The psychologist is not primarily focused on the individual student (though they care about the student). The psychologist is focused on building the consultee's — usually the teacher's — capacity, skills, and confidence. Think of it this way: give a teacher a solution for one student, and they can handle that one student. Teach a teacher a strategy for solving behavioral or academic problems broadly, and they can handle dozens of students over their career. Consultee-centered consultation is an investment in the teacher's professional growth. It reduces long-term dependence on the psychologist and scales the impact far beyond one case. On the Praxis exam, when a question emphasizes "building the teacher's skills," "reducing dependence on the school psychologist," or "developing the consultee's capacity," the answer is consultee-centered consultation.

## First-Principles Explanation

Consultee-centered consultation is one of four models of consultation described by Gerald Caplan, a pioneer in consultation psychology. The four models differ in their primary focus:

1. **Client-centered consultation:** Focus is on solving the client's (student's) problem. Example: "How do we help this student improve reading?"

2. **Consultee-centered consultation:** Focus is on developing the consultee's (teacher's) skills, knowledge, and problem-solving capacity. Example: "How do we help this teacher understand reading skill gaps so she can address them for any student?"

3. **Program-centered consultation:** Focus is on improving a program, policy, or system. Example: "How do we improve the school-wide reading curriculum?"

4. **Administrative consultation:** Focus is on supporting organizational decision-making and leadership. Example: "How do we structure professional development in the school?"

**Consultee-centered consultation specifics:**

Goals:
- Increase the consultee's understanding of their own role and impact
- Expand the consultee's knowledge of psychological concepts and strategies
- Build the consultee's problem-solving and observation skills
- Increase the consultee's confidence and sense of efficacy
- Reduce dependence on external experts

Methods:
- **Psychoeducation:** Teaching the teacher about child development, learning disorders, behavior theory, so they have a deeper understanding
- **Reflective questions:** Asking the teacher to think deeply about the student and their own responses, rather than just telling them
- **Observation and feedback:** Helping the teacher see patterns in their own teaching or classroom management
- **Skill-building:** Teaching specific strategies for assessment, classroom management, instruction
- **Follow-up and coaching:** Supporting the teacher over time as they develop new skills

**Underlying principle:** The consultee (teacher) is the expert on their own classroom and students. The consultant (psychologist) is a facilitator who helps the teacher think more deeply, gain new knowledge, and develop new skills. The relationship is collaborative and growth-oriented.

**Client-centered vs. Consultee-centered — key difference:**

- **Client-centered:** "Your student has a reading disability. Here is a specific intervention plan for him."
- **Consultee-centered:** "Let me teach you how to recognize the signs of a reading disability and adjust your instruction for students with those signs. What strategies do you already use? What might we build on?"

In client-centered, the focus is narrow: one student, one problem, one plan. In consultee-centered, the focus is broad: the teacher's skills, applicable to many students.

## Worked Example

**Scenario 1 — Client-Centered Consultation:**

A third-grade teacher, Ms. Garcia, refers a student, Carlos, for behavior problems (off-task, disruptive). The school psychologist:

1. Conducts an FBA with the teacher's input
2. Identifies that Carlos's off-task behavior is driven by task avoidance when work is difficult
3. Recommends a specific plan for Carlos: simplified worksheet, preferential seating, positive reinforcement for task completion

Ms. Garcia implements the plan. Carlos improves. Success!

Six months later, another student, Diego, shows similar behavior patterns. Ms. Garcia refers Diego to the school psychologist. The cycle repeats.

**Limitation:** Ms. Garcia has solved one student's problem but has not developed her own skill in recognizing task-avoidance behavior or designing interventions. She remains dependent on the psychologist.

**Scenario 2 — Consultee-Centered Consultation:**

Same initial referral: Carlos is off-task and disruptive. But the consultation takes a different approach.

**Psychologist:** "Before we develop a plan for Carlos, I would like to understand your perspective. What do you notice about when Carlos is off-task? What is he doing or avoiding?"

**Ms. Garcia:** "He gets frustrated when the math is hard and just shuts down. He does not ask for help."

**Psychologist:** "That is a really keen observation. You have identified something important: the trigger (hard math) and the behavior (shutdown instead of help-seeking). In behavior terms, we call this avoidance. The function is escaping the difficult task."

**Ms. Garcia:** "So it is not that he does not want to learn; he is just avoiding the struggle?"

**Psychologist:** "Exactly. And avoidance behavior gets reinforced when the task goes away or becomes easier. Let me teach you about how to recognize avoidance behavior and some general strategies that work across multiple students."

The psychologist spends 20 minutes teaching:
- How to distinguish avoidance (behavior drops when task gets harder) from other behaviors (like attention-seeking, which increases with difficulty)
- Three strategies for addressing avoidance: (a) make the task easier initially, (b) teach the student how to ask for help, (c) reinforce task-initiation
- How to observe and track whether the student's avoidance is decreasing

**Ms. Garcia:** "I see. So if I reduce the problem difficulty and teach Carlos to ask for help, I might reduce his avoidance. But I should try this approach with other students too?"

**Psychologist:** "Yes. The same logic applies to any student who avoids difficult tasks. Let's develop a plan for Carlos, but more importantly, you now have a framework for thinking about this pattern in your classroom."

**Follow-up:** Two weeks later, the psychologist checks in with Ms. Garcia. She reports that Carlos is improving and, notably, she has recognized similar avoidance patterns in two other students and has tried the simplified task + help-seeking teaching with them. Both are showing improvement.

**Result:** Ms. Garcia has not just solved Carlos's problem; she has developed a framework for recognizing and intervening on avoidance behavior that applies to multiple students. She has become more independent and skilled.

One year later, when a new student shows avoidance, Ms. Garcia recognizes it and designs an intervention without referring to the psychologist. The impact of the consultee-centered consultation has compounded.

## Named Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Consultee-centered consultation means not helping the current student."**
Reality: Consultee-centered consultation includes helping the current client (student), but it situates that help within a broader goal of building the consultee's capacity. It is not either-or; it is both.

**Misconception 2: "Student-centered consultation and consultee-centered consultation are the same thing."**
Reality: Student-centered focuses on the individual student's problem. Consultee-centered focuses on the teacher's growth and skills, with the student's problem as a vehicle for that growth.

**Misconception 3: "Consultee-centered consultation requires more time and is inefficient."**
Reality: Initial time investment is slightly higher, but the long-term payoff is enormous. A teacher who develops skills can handle dozens of future cases without the psychologist. It is more efficient overall.

**Misconception 4: "All teachers want consultee-centered consultation; some just want a solution."**
Reality: Some teachers are overwhelmed and genuinely need a quick, specific solution for today. Good consultee-centered consultation meets them where they are, offers the solution, AND does some brief skill-building if the teacher is open to it.

## If You Only Remember One Thing

**Consultee-centered consultation = developing the teacher's skills to handle not just this problem, but similar problems in the future. It scales impact and reduces dependence.**

## Analogy

**Consultee-centered is "teach a person to fish"; client-centered is "give them fish."** One solves today's hunger; the other builds a skill for a lifetime.

## Concept-Specific Mini-Quiz Stems

1. **Stem:** A school psychologist is consulting with a teacher about a student's reading disability. The goal is to help the teacher understand reading skill gaps in general so she can modify her instruction for any student with similar patterns. This is an example of:
   **Correct answer:** Consultee-centered consultation. The focus is on building the teacher's knowledge and skills (recognizing reading gaps, modifying instruction) rather than just solving one student's problem.

2. **Stem:** A teacher says to the psychologist, "I have three students with similar behavior problems. I do not understand what they have in common." The psychologist's best response in a consultee-centered model is:
   **Correct answer:** Help the teacher observe and identify the pattern themselves (perhaps through guided questioning), then teach the conceptual framework (e.g., "These students all show avoidance behavior when tasks are difficult"). This builds the teacher's capacity to recognize and address the pattern independently in the future.

3. **Stem:** After a brief consultee-centered consultation about identifying and addressing avoidance behavior, the teacher says, "I think I see this pattern in a new student. Can I try the intervention myself before calling you?" The psychologist should:
   **Correct answer:** Encourage the teacher to try it, offer brief coaching if needed, and follow up to see how it went. This is the goal of consultee-centered consultation — the teacher becomes independent and confident in addressing similar problems.
