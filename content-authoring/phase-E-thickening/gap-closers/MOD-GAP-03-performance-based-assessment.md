# MOD-GAP-03: Performance-Based Assessment (Portfolios and Work Samples)

*Closes ETS gap I.A.2.f — "Understands appropriate use and interpretation of performance-based assessment (e.g., work samples, portfolios)." Proposed new skill: **DBD-11 (Performance-Based Assessment)**.*

## In Plain Language

Performance-based assessment is what you learn about a student by looking at what they actually *produce* — a writing portfolio, a lab report, a math journal, a video of a presentation — rather than what they score on a single standardized test. A standardized test gives you a number that compares a student to thousands of peers on a tightly scoped set of items. A portfolio gives you a window into the student's thinking across weeks or months, in their own voice, on authentic tasks. Both are legitimate. Neither replaces the other. On the Praxis, performance-based assessment shows up as the answer when the question asks about *authentic*, *curriculum-embedded*, or *progress-over-time* evidence that norm-referenced tests cannot provide.

## First-Principles Explanation

Performance-based assessment (PBA) rests on a single premise: **the best evidence of what a student can do is what the student has actually done**. A multiple-choice reading test measures how well a student answers reading questions on a 45-minute test; it does not measure whether the student can write a coherent essay, synthesize across texts, or sustain analysis across a week-long project. PBA fills that gap.

Three formats dominate the category:

1. **Work samples.** A single piece of student-produced work — a writing sample, a completed math problem set, a science poster — scored against a rubric. Work samples are fast to collect and easy to compare against specific criteria. They are the everyday evidence teachers already generate.

2. **Portfolios.** A curated collection of work samples over time, usually including a mix of best-work and process pieces, sometimes with student reflection attached. Portfolios show growth, not just snapshots. They are most informative when students participate in selection and reflection.

3. **Performance tasks.** Structured, often timed tasks where students demonstrate a complex skill — deliver a speech, conduct an experiment, solve a multi-step real-world problem. Scored with well-constructed rubrics, performance tasks capture application and synthesis, which standardized tests rarely do well.

The trade-off with PBA is **reliability versus authenticity**. A norm-referenced test score has high reliability (two administrations produce similar scores) but limited authenticity (it does not look like classroom work). A portfolio has high authenticity (it *is* classroom work) but lower reliability (two raters may score it differently). The school psychologist's job is to know when each type of evidence matters.

On the Praxis, PBA typically shows up in three scenarios:

- **RTI / progress monitoring.** CBM data is the primary progress metric, but work samples and portfolios supply the qualitative evidence that shows whether a student's skills are generalizing to real tasks. A student can score on CBM without being able to produce a coherent paragraph.
- **Eligibility determinations.** Work samples document classroom functioning and support triangulation with norm-referenced scores (e.g., a discrepancy between a student's WIAT-4 reading score and their classroom writing samples is clinically meaningful).
- **Culturally and linguistically diverse students.** Where standardized tests may be biased, work samples and portfolios provide authentic evidence of what the student can do in the curriculum of record, particularly when scored by raters familiar with the student's cultural and linguistic context.

A final technical note: the reliability problem can be mitigated. Well-constructed **analytic rubrics**, rater training, and calibration sessions can bring inter-rater reliability into acceptable ranges (Cohen's kappa > .70 or intraclass correlation coefficient > .80). PBA without a rubric is subjective; PBA with a well-validated rubric is defensible.

## Worked Example

Ms. Alvarez, a school psychologist, is conducting a reevaluation for Diego, a 4th grader currently served under a Specific Learning Disability eligibility for reading. His most recent WIAT-4 Reading Comprehension subtest yielded a Standard Score of 82 (12th percentile) — an improvement from his initial eligibility score of 74.

A number alone does not tell the IEP team enough. Ms. Alvarez requests Diego's **reading portfolio** from his teacher. The portfolio contains: three reading-response journal entries over the quarter, two graphic organizers completed after read-alouds, and a book-report project.

Reviewing the portfolio against a grade-level rubric, she finds: Diego's journal entries show increasing use of text-based evidence across three months (growth); his graphic organizers are accurately completed when scaffolded but sparse when unscaffolded (supports still needed); his book report demonstrates main-idea identification but not inference across paragraphs.

Her eligibility conclusion triangulates both sources: WIAT-4 shows measurable improvement in standardized reading; the portfolio shows that improvement is real in the classroom but still dependent on scaffolds. The IEP team continues services, shifts the goals toward inference and independent strategy use, and documents the portfolio evidence in the evaluation report.

Without the portfolio, the WIAT-4 number alone might have prompted either unwarranted reduction in services (the score is "improving") or unwarranted increase (the score is still "below average"). The portfolio provided the classroom context that converted a number into a decision.

## Named Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Portfolios are subjective and not defensible for IEP decisions."**
Reality: Portfolios scored with a well-constructed analytic rubric and with documented inter-rater reliability are defensible. The key is structure, not the medium. Portfolios *without* a rubric are indeed subjective. The question on the Praxis is rarely "Use or do not use portfolios" — it is "Use portfolios correctly."

**Misconception 2: "Work samples are inferior to standardized tests."**
Reality: They measure different things. A student's classroom work samples are often the strongest evidence of what the student *actually does* in the curriculum. Standardized tests provide comparison to peers. Neither is inferior; they answer different questions. Eligibility and programming decisions are strongest when multiple sources converge.

**Misconception 3: "Performance tasks take too long to be practical in RTI/MTSS."**
Reality: Short, well-designed performance tasks embedded in regular instruction can generate progress-monitoring data efficiently. A 5-minute written retell, a 3-minute math word-problem sample, a 1-minute oral reading fluency probe paired with a 1-minute comprehension response — all are performance-based and routinely feasible.

## If You Only Remember One Thing

**Performance-based assessment is the authentic evidence — work samples, portfolios, performance tasks — that complements standardized scores. Triangulation of both produces defensible educational decisions. Either source used alone is vulnerable to error.**

## Analogy

Standardized test scores are like a **blood-pressure reading** — a single, precise number taken at a single moment, comparable across patients, but telling you nothing about diet, sleep, or stress across the month. A portfolio is the **monthly health journal** — messier, qualitative, but showing the patterns that the single number cannot. Doctors use both. So should school psychologists.

## Concept-Specific Mini-Quiz Stems

1. **Stem:** A school psychologist is reviewing eligibility data for a 5th-grade student and notices that the WIAT-4 reading score is in the low-average range (SS 85) but the classroom teacher reports the student produces coherent, grade-level writing samples. What should the school psychologist do next?
   **Answer:** Collect and review the classroom work samples using a grade-level rubric to triangulate the standardized score. Discrepancies between standardized and performance-based evidence are clinically meaningful and warrant structured review rather than dismissal of either source.

2. **Stem:** An IEP team is debating whether portfolio evidence is admissible as part of an eligibility decision. What guidance most accurately reflects best practice?
   **Answer:** Portfolio evidence is admissible and valuable when scored against a well-constructed analytic rubric with documented inter-rater reliability, and when it supplements — rather than replaces — other sources of evidence.

3. **Stem:** For a student from a culturally and linguistically diverse background being evaluated for a specific learning disability, why might performance-based assessment add meaningful information beyond the standardized battery?
   **Answer:** Performance-based assessment captures how the student engages with the curriculum of record in their own voice, is less susceptible to cultural and linguistic biases embedded in standardized instruments, and provides authentic evidence of classroom functioning that complements norm-referenced scores.
