# MOD-GAP-08: Assessment Technology and Data Tools

*Closes ETS gap I.A.2.i — "Knows how to use information and technology resources to enhance data collection and decision making." Proposed new skill: **DBD-13 (Assessment Technology and Data Tools)**.*

## In Plain Language

Modern school psychology practice runs on data systems. A school psychologist who can only hand-score paper protocols and maintain a filing cabinet of test booklets is working at a fraction of their potential. **Assessment technology** refers to the digital tools that collect, store, analyze, and visualize student data — from computerized curriculum-based measurement (CBM) platforms to RTI/MTSS dashboards to electronic assessment administration. The Praxis does not expect you to know any specific vendor's product line, but it does expect you to know **what categories of tools exist, what they are used for, and what the ethical and technical pitfalls are**.

## First-Principles Explanation

The first principle: **data are only useful if they can be accessed, analyzed, and acted upon quickly enough to drive decisions**. A benchmark score buried in a file drawer is invisible to the team making Tier 2 placement decisions next week. The value of assessment technology lies in turning raw data into actionable information — and doing so in a way that respects student privacy and psychometric integrity.

Four categories of tools dominate the field:

**1. Universal screening and progress-monitoring platforms.** Computerized CBM tools — *aimsweb Plus, STAR Assessments (Renaissance), FastBridge (formerly FAST), DIBELS Data System, easyCBM* — administer brief academic probes (oral reading fluency, math computation, early literacy indicators) and generate graphed progress data automatically. They provide national norms, cut-scores for risk identification, and automated goal-line projections. The school psychologist uses these to identify students below benchmark, set progress-monitoring goals, and evaluate response to intervention.

**2. Behavioral and social-emotional screening tools.** *BASC-3 BESS, SRSS-IE, SAEBRS, Panorama Social-Emotional Learning Survey, Devereux Student Strengths Assessment (DESSA)*. These platforms administer universal screeners, compile classroom- and school-level reports, and flag students at elevated risk. Many integrate with student information systems (SIS) so that demographic, attendance, and discipline data can be layered on.

**3. RTI/MTSS management systems.** *Branching Minds, Panorama Student Success, Illuminate, PowerSchool Unified Insights.* These are decision-support dashboards that aggregate screening, progress-monitoring, attendance, behavior, and intervention-history data at the student, grade, and school level. They are the backbone of data-based decision making at scale.

**4. Electronic assessment administration.** *Q-global (Pearson), Q-interactive (Pearson, iPad-based WISC-V / WIAT administration), WJIV Online Scoring (Riverside), Conners-4 online.* These platforms allow secure administration, scoring, and report generation. The psychometric content is the same as the paper version, but administration is standardized through software.

A school psychologist working at an expert level:

- **Pulls data from the SIS** rather than asking teachers to compile spreadsheets.
- **Interprets progress-monitoring graphs** using trend lines, aimlines, and the 4-point rule (four consecutive data points below the aimline → intervention change warranted).
- **Uses screening-plus-intervention-history data** to generate defensible Tier 2/3 placement recommendations.
- **Recognizes platform limitations** — a dashboard's risk flags are as valid as the underlying norms and cut-scores; a student flagged by one platform may not be flagged by another.

Two ethical anchors are non-negotiable:

**FERPA (Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act).** Any digital platform that stores student-identifiable data is covered by FERPA. Data-sharing agreements with vendors must specify who owns the data, how it is stored, how it is destroyed, and under what circumstances it may be disclosed. Free or trial tools that ingest student data without a formal agreement are a FERPA risk.

**Data security and minimum necessary access.** Role-based access (teacher vs. specialist vs. administrator) should align with the minimum necessary principle — teachers see their classrooms, case managers see their caseloads, administrators see aggregates. Uploading raw protocol data to an uncontrolled cloud (Google Drive, Dropbox) without encryption or access controls is a violation of best practice even when the platform itself is secure.

## Worked Example

Dr. Aldana is hired as the MTSS coordinator for a medium-sized elementary district. The schools have been using paper-and-pencil DIBELS for reading screening, and each principal maintains a separate Excel spreadsheet with results. There is no central data system, and every Tier 2 decision takes weeks of back-and-forth.

Dr. Aldana's plan spans three phases:

**Phase 1 (infrastructure):** Procure a single computerized CBM platform district-wide. She evaluates *aimsweb Plus, FastBridge, and easyCBM* on three criteria: psychometric quality (reliability, national norms), teacher usability (time to administer, report clarity), and integration (does it export to the SIS?). She selects FastBridge.

**Phase 2 (implementation):** Train teachers on administration protocols, set up teacher-level dashboards, configure automated reports for grade-level team meetings. She works with IT and the superintendent's office to execute a FERPA-compliant data-sharing agreement with Illuminate (the vendor).

**Phase 3 (decision support):** She overlays FastBridge data with attendance, behavior referrals, and EL status from the SIS. A student with below-benchmark reading who also has 15% absences and 3 ODRs looks very different from a student with the same reading score and no other risk factors. The MTSS team uses this layered view to differentiate Tier 2 and Tier 3 placements.

A year later, the district's Tier 2 placement rate has become defensible, grade-level teams are running data-driven meetings in 25 minutes instead of 75, and Tier 3 referrals are up 40% — not because students are sicker, but because students who had been invisible in paper-and-pencil systems are now being seen.

## Named Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "The platform's risk flag is the diagnosis."**
Reality: Screening platforms flag *risk*, not disability. A flag is a signal that warrants follow-up — closer progress monitoring, a diagnostic assessment, a team conversation — not an independent judgment of identification. Treating flags as diagnoses creates overidentification and legal exposure.

**Misconception 2: "If it's free and on the internet, we can use it."**
Reality: Free-to-teacher tools often are not FERPA-compliant at the institutional level. Before adopting any platform that will receive student-identifiable data, the district needs a data-sharing agreement specifying ownership, storage, breach notification, and destruction. "Convenient" is not a legal standard.

**Misconception 3: "More data is always better."**
Reality: Data quality matters more than data quantity. A system that produces a dashboard with 40 indicators per student but no clear decision rules is worse than a system with 6 well-chosen indicators and clear Tier 2/3 criteria. The goal is *actionable* data — data that drives a next step — not data for its own sake.

## If You Only Remember One Thing

**Assessment technology is not about the tool — it is about the decision the tool enables. A system that does not connect data to a next action is decorative, not functional. And every digital tool that touches student data is a FERPA decision.**

## Analogy

Assessment technology is to school psychology what **electronic medical records** are to clinical medicine: a necessary infrastructure for coordinated care at scale. The EMR does not diagnose the patient; the physician does. But without the EMR, the physician cannot see the patient's full history, and care fragments. The school psychologist's role with assessment tech is the physician's role with the EMR — use it to see the whole student, and never confuse the tool's alerts with professional judgment.

## Concept-Specific Mini-Quiz Stems

1. **Stem:** A universal-screening platform flags a student as "high risk" for reading difficulty. Which of the following is the *most appropriate* next step for the school psychologist?
   **Answer:** Treat the flag as a signal that warrants additional diagnostic assessment and data-team review — not as a diagnostic conclusion — and layer in attendance, behavior, language status, and classroom performance data before recommending a tier change.

2. **Stem:** A district is considering adopting a free online SEL screening tool to administer to all students. Before proceeding, which of the following considerations is *most* important?
   **Answer:** A FERPA-compliant data-sharing agreement with the vendor specifying ownership, storage, access, and destruction of student-identifiable data, along with verification of the tool's psychometric quality and appropriateness for the population.

3. **Stem:** A school psychologist is reviewing a student's progress-monitoring graph from a computerized CBM platform. After four consecutive data points falling below the aimline, what is the *most appropriate* next step?
   **Answer:** Convene the intervention team to review fidelity of the current intervention, consider intensifying or changing the intervention, and document the decision — consistent with the standard four-point decision rule in progress monitoring.
