# MOD-GAP-04: ESSA (Every Student Succeeds Act)

*Closes ETS gap IV.C.2.a — "Knows the major federal laws and regulations governing the practice of school psychology (e.g., FERPA, Section 504, **ESSA**, IDEA and its eligibility categories)." ESSA is named explicitly by ETS but had no dedicated coverage in the 45-skill map. Proposed new skill / sub-concept: **LEG-05 (ESSA)** or a sub-concept under LEG-02.*

## In Plain Language

ESSA — the Every Student Succeeds Act of 2015 — is the federal law that controls how public K-12 schools are held accountable. It replaced No Child Left Behind (NCLB, 2001) and reversed the federal government's top-down approach: under NCLB, Washington set the rules and punished failing schools; under ESSA, states design their own accountability plans and the federal role shrinks to approval and oversight. For a school psychologist, three pieces of ESSA show up again and again: the four-tier evidence ladder (required when using Title I funds for interventions), universal screening expectations built into state plans, and a much stronger emphasis on school climate, social-emotional learning, and non-academic indicators as part of how schools are judged.

## First-Principles Explanation

ESSA was signed in December 2015 and fully replaced NCLB beginning with the 2017–2018 school year. It is the current reauthorization of the **Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA)** of 1965 — the same foundational law, amended every decade or so. Knowing this lineage matters: when the Praxis says "ESSA," it means the current federal K–12 education law.

Three structural features define ESSA:

**1. State-designed accountability systems.** Each state submits an ESSA plan to the U.S. Department of Education. Plans must include: challenging academic standards, annual assessments in reading and math (grades 3–8 and once in high school), a school accountability system, and intervention requirements for low-performing schools. But states pick the specific indicators, the weights, and the intervention strategies. This is the big structural shift from NCLB: accountability is state-designed, not federally prescribed.

**2. Multiple indicators, including a non-academic one.** Under ESSA, state accountability systems must include at least one indicator of **school quality or student success that is not an academic achievement measure** — commonly chronic absenteeism, school climate, student engagement, or access to advanced coursework. This is the lever that pulled school psychology, social-emotional learning, and school-climate work directly into accountability conversations.

**3. Evidence-based intervention tiers.** When Title I schools are identified for **Comprehensive Support and Improvement (CSI)** or **Targeted Support and Improvement (TSI)**, they must use **evidence-based interventions**. ESSA defines a four-tier evidence hierarchy:

- **Tier 1 — Strong evidence:** at least one well-designed, well-implemented experimental study.
- **Tier 2 — Moderate evidence:** at least one well-designed, well-implemented quasi-experimental study.
- **Tier 3 — Promising evidence:** at least one well-designed, well-implemented correlational study with statistical controls.
- **Tier 4 — Demonstrates a rationale:** logic model based on research, evaluation planned.

These tiers show up whenever ESSA funds are used. School psychologists are expected to help districts identify which interventions meet which tier — the *What Works Clearinghouse* and *Evidence for ESSA* are the two standard vetted sources.

On the Praxis, ESSA typically shows up alongside IDEA and Section 504. The key distinction: **IDEA is individual special-education entitlement law, Section 504 is individual civil-rights / accommodation law, ESSA is system-level accountability and funding law.** All three can apply to the same student but they answer different questions.

## Worked Example

Jefferson Middle School has been flagged for **Targeted Support and Improvement** (TSI) under their state's ESSA plan because one subgroup — English learners — is consistently underperforming on state reading assessments. The district superintendent asks Dr. Patel, the school psychologist, to help the school leadership team select a reading intervention for this subgroup using Title I funds.

Dr. Patel walks the team through the ESSA evidence ladder. They review three candidate interventions:

- **Option A:** A computer-based reading program that has a single randomized controlled trial (RCT) showing significant effects on English learners' reading scores, well-implemented. — **Tier 1 (Strong).**
- **Option B:** A small-group reading intervention with a quasi-experimental study showing effects but no RCT. — **Tier 2 (Moderate).**
- **Option C:** A new program the vendor describes as "research-based" but which has only a theoretical logic model and no completed outcome study. — **Tier 4 (Demonstrates a rationale).**

Because ESSA requires evidence-based interventions for TSI schools and Title I funds, Dr. Patel recommends the team proceed with Option A or B and include an evaluation plan. Option C is permissible under Tier 4 but would require a concurrent formal evaluation and a defensible rationale — appropriate only if the stronger options are not feasible.

She also reminds the team that the non-academic indicator in their state's plan is chronic absenteeism, so any ESSA-funded intervention package should consider attendance supports — not because Dr. Patel thinks they are nice to have, but because they are part of how the school will be judged next year.

## Named Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "ESSA is just NCLB with a new name."**
Reality: The names are in the same lineage but the federalism structure is fundamentally different. NCLB prescribed federal sanctions for failing schools; ESSA devolves plan design and intervention strategy to states. Under NCLB, all students had to be "proficient" by 2014; ESSA eliminated that deadline and replaced it with state-defined long-term goals. Praxis items that test ESSA often contrast it with NCLB.

**Misconception 2: "Only Tier 1 (Strong) evidence is acceptable under ESSA."**
Reality: All four tiers are permissible under ESSA, but the tier required depends on funding stream and school status. CSI/TSI Title I interventions have to meet at least one of the first three tiers; Tier 4 (rationale + evaluation plan) is a legal backstop for innovation. The test trap is treating ESSA as "only RCT-supported interventions are legal" — that is NCLB-era thinking.

**Misconception 3: "ESSA replaces IDEA for students with disabilities."**
Reality: ESSA and IDEA are different laws that coexist. ESSA governs accountability and general-education funding; IDEA governs special-education entitlement and FAPE. A student with an IEP is covered by both. ESSA also does not override Section 504. All three laws can apply to the same student simultaneously.

## If You Only Remember One Thing

**ESSA = state-designed accountability + four-tier evidence ladder + at least one non-academic indicator. It replaced NCLB in 2015. It coexists with IDEA and Section 504 — it does not replace them.**

## Analogy

NCLB was the federal government as **head coach**, calling every play. ESSA is the federal government as **commissioner**, setting the rulebook and approving team game plans but letting each state run its own offense. Individual civil-rights protections (IDEA, 504) are the league-wide safety rules that apply no matter which offense a state runs.

## Concept-Specific Mini-Quiz Stems

1. **Stem:** A district is selecting a Tier 2 reading intervention to be funded under Title I for a school identified for Targeted Support and Improvement. Which of the following evidence profiles would *most clearly* satisfy the ESSA requirement for "strong" evidence?
   **Answer:** A well-designed, well-implemented randomized controlled trial showing significant effects of the intervention on the targeted outcome for a population comparable to the school's students.

2. **Stem:** Which federal law's design most strongly shapes how states choose to *measure school climate* as part of school accountability?
   **Answer:** ESSA (which requires at least one non-academic indicator of school quality or student success in each state's accountability plan).

3. **Stem:** How does ESSA most clearly differ from NCLB in its approach to school accountability?
   **Answer:** ESSA gives states primary responsibility for designing accountability systems and selecting interventions, whereas NCLB imposed federally prescribed sanctions and required universal proficiency by 2014.
