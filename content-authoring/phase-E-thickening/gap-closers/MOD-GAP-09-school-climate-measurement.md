# MOD-GAP-09: School Climate Measurement and Evaluation

*Closes ETS gap III.B.5 — "Is familiar with effective methods to measure and evaluate school safety and school climate." Proposed new skill: **SSV-09 (School Climate Measurement and Evaluation)**.*

## In Plain Language

**School climate** is the felt quality of a school — how safe students feel, how respected they feel, how connected to adults and peers they feel, and how much they believe the school is fair and orderly. It is not a vague concept. Climate is measured with validated surveys, behavioral indicators, and observational data, and the measurements matter — climate scores predict attendance, achievement, dropout, bullying rates, and teacher retention. For a school psychologist, climate work sits at the intersection of Tier 1 prevention and systems-level consultation. And under ESSA, at least one non-academic indicator is required in every state's accountability plan — which means climate data now carries consequences for schools that ignore it.

## First-Principles Explanation

The first principle: **climate is a construct that requires multiple measures to capture**. No single metric tells the whole story. Best practice is triangulation — survey data (what people say), behavioral data (what people do), and observational data (what a trained observer sees).

Three dimensions are typically measured (per the U.S. Department of Education's ED School Climate Surveys framework):

**1. Engagement.** Relationships, respect for diversity, school participation. Typical items: "Adults at this school care about me." "Students at this school respect each other's differences." High-engagement schools have lower dropout and higher achievement.

**2. Safety.** Physical safety, emotional safety, bullying, substance use. Typical items: "I feel safe at school." "I have been bullied at school in the past month." Safety items are often disaggregated by demographic subgroup — national data consistently show that LGBTQ+ students, students with disabilities, and students of color report lower safety scores in many schools.

**3. Environment.** Physical condition, academic environment, discipline fairness, wellness. Typical items: "The rules are fair." "The building is clean and well-maintained." "My teachers have high expectations for me."

Validated instruments include:

- **ED School Climate Surveys (EDSCLS)** — free, federally validated, three versions (student, staff, family), aligned to the three dimensions above.
- **Panorama Education School Climate Survey** — widely used commercial platform with benchmark comparisons and subgroup disaggregation.
- **California Healthy Kids Survey (CHKS) / School Climate Survey** — statewide implementation model, used widely in research.
- **Georgia School Climate Survey, Delaware School Climate Survey** — state-specific validated tools.

**Behavioral/archival indicators** triangulate survey data:

- **Attendance and chronic absenteeism** (≥ 10% of enrolled days missed — often the ESSA non-academic indicator).
- **Office discipline referrals (ODRs)**, disaggregated by race, disability, and grade — a leading indicator of climate problems, especially if ODRs are concentrated in specific settings (cafeteria, buses, hallways) or specific student subgroups.
- **Suspensions and expulsions**, disaggregated — disproportionality is itself a climate signal.
- **Bullying reports** (note: report rates reflect both prevalence and comfort reporting).
- **Teacher turnover and staff survey data.**

**Observational tools** include the **School-Wide Evaluation Tool (SET)** for PBIS fidelity, and walkthrough protocols for physical environment audits.

Two measurement principles matter on the Praxis:

**Disaggregation is not optional.** A school-wide average masks subgroup experiences. A school with a 75% "feel safe" score may have a 90% score among one subgroup and a 50% score among another. Climate work that does not disaggregate by race, gender, disability status, LGBTQ+ identity, and EL status misses the students most likely to be harmed.

**Climate data drives action, not reporting.** A climate survey that is administered, reported to the board, and then shelved has failed. The test-relevant cycle is *measure → share with stakeholders → develop action plan → implement → re-measure*. Climate improvement takes 2–3 years to show in survey data and is typically tied to Tier 1 SEL, PBIS, restorative practices, or trauma-informed systems changes.

## Worked Example

Central Middle School has been flagged in the district's ESSA plan for declining chronic-absenteeism rates (from 12% to 21% over three years). The assistant superintendent asks Dr. Patel, the district school psychologist, to lead a climate assessment and action plan.

Dr. Patel designs a triangulated assessment:

- **Survey layer.** She administers EDSCLS to all students in grades 6–8, all staff, and a stratified family sample. She ensures surveys are available in the three primary home languages of the school.
- **Archival layer.** She pulls three years of attendance by subgroup, ODRs disaggregated by location and race, suspension rates, and bullying reports.
- **Observational layer.** She walks the building during transitions with the assistant principal and completes a SET assessment of the existing PBIS framework.

The data converge on a pattern: engagement scores are high in classrooms with experienced teachers, but plummet during unstructured time (cafeteria, hallways, bus line). ODRs are heavily concentrated in these same settings, and are disproportionately assigned to Black boys and students with disabilities. Family survey data show concerns about communication with the school and transportation reliability.

Dr. Patel presents three action priorities to the leadership team:

1. **Unstructured-time redesign.** Add structured cafeteria activities, staff coverage in hallways, and restorative conversations in place of reflexive ODRs for minor infractions.
2. **Tier 1 SEL refresh.** The existing SEL curriculum has low fidelity data. Retrain, coach, and add fidelity monitoring.
3. **Family engagement plan.** Monthly multilingual communication, transportation-barrier survey, and a family advisory panel.

She proposes remeasuring climate in 12 months with the same instruments, with a target of a 5-point improvement on the engagement and safety subscales and a 3-point reduction in chronic absenteeism.

A year later, the subgroup scores that were lowest have moved the most, the ODR disproportionality has narrowed, and the chronic absenteeism rate has stabilized at 16%. The work is not done, but the loop is working.

## Named Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "A climate survey is the climate assessment."**
Reality: Surveys are one layer. A defensible climate assessment triangulates survey, behavioral/archival, and observational data. Surveys alone capture perception but not patterns — a school where students feel safe on average may have serious safety problems concentrated in a subgroup that the average masks.

**Misconception 2: "Climate data should be kept confidential to protect the school."**
Reality: Climate data that are not shared with stakeholders — students, staff, families — cannot drive action. The test-relevant cycle includes transparent reporting, at least at the school level and with appropriate disaggregation. Protecting individual privacy is a separate requirement, handled through aggregation and cell-size rules (typically suppressing cells with n < 10).

**Misconception 3: "Climate improvement should be visible within a year."**
Reality: Climate change is slow. Survey effects from Tier 1 interventions (PBIS, SEL, trauma-informed practices) typically require 2–3 years to stabilize. A program with weak year-one effects may be on track — the judgment depends on implementation fidelity, not just outcomes.

## If You Only Remember One Thing

**Triangulate. One data source is not a climate assessment. Surveys + behavioral/archival + observational, disaggregated by subgroup, with an action plan tied to the findings and a remeasurement plan — that is climate work.**

## Analogy

Measuring school climate is like taking a **medical history and vitals on a patient**. The survey is the interview (what the patient reports). The attendance, ODR, and suspension data are the vitals (what the body is doing). The walkthrough is the physical exam (what the clinician observes). No competent physician relies on one of these alone, and no competent school psychologist relies on one climate data source alone.

## Visual Primitive

**Recommendation: A triangulation triangle plus a disaggregation matrix.**

- **Triangle:** three vertices labeled Survey (engagement, safety, environment), Behavioral/Archival (attendance, ODRs, suspensions, bullying reports), Observational (walkthroughs, SET, fidelity checks). Inside the triangle: "Triangulated Climate Picture."
- **Disaggregation matrix:** a grid with subgroups (race/ethnicity, gender, disability, LGBTQ+, EL, SES) as rows and climate indicators (safety, engagement, environment, chronic absence, ODRs) as columns. Cells show whether each combination has been examined. Empty cells are a gap.

## Concept-Specific Mini-Quiz Stems

1. **Stem:** A school administers a school climate survey and reports an overall "feel safe at school" score of 78%. What is the *most appropriate* next analytical step for the school psychologist?
   **Answer:** Disaggregate the score by demographic subgroups (race/ethnicity, gender, disability status, LGBTQ+ identity, EL status) to identify whether subgroup experiences differ substantially from the school-wide average, since subgroup disparities are obscured by overall averages.

2. **Stem:** Which of the following combinations *best* constitutes a triangulated school climate assessment?
   **Answer:** A validated school climate survey administered to students, staff, and families; archival review of attendance, office discipline referrals, and suspensions disaggregated by subgroup; and observational walkthroughs or fidelity assessments of existing schoolwide frameworks.

3. **Stem:** Under ESSA, each state's accountability plan must include at least one indicator of school quality or student success that is not an academic achievement measure. Which of the following is a commonly used such indicator that also functions as a climate indicator?
   **Answer:** Chronic absenteeism — the proportion of students missing at least 10% of enrolled school days — which is both an ESSA-eligible non-academic indicator and a validated leading indicator of school climate and engagement.
