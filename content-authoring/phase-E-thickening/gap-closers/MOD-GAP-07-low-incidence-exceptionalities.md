# MOD-GAP-07: Assessment of Low-Incidence Exceptionalities

*Closes ETS gap I.A.4.c — "Is familiar with the assessment of students with low-incidence exceptionalities (e.g., chronic health impairments, severe physical disabilities, sensory impairments)." Proposed new skill: **DBD-12 (Low-Incidence Exceptionalities)** or extension under ACA-09.*

## In Plain Language

Low-incidence exceptionalities are the disability categories that most school psychologists encounter only a handful of times in a career — students who are **deaf or hard of hearing, blind or visually impaired, deafblind, severely orthopedically impaired, or have significant chronic health impairments** that substantially affect school functioning. The hallmark of assessment with these students is that **standard cognitive tests are usually not valid without specialized adaptation or alternatives**. A student who cannot hear the verbal instructions cannot be meaningfully scored on a traditional verbal IQ subtest. A student who cannot see the stimulus book cannot complete the nonverbal matrix items. The school psychologist's job is not to administer the standard battery; it is to know when the standard battery does not apply and who to bring in.

## First-Principles Explanation

The first principle: **most standardized intelligence and achievement tests were normed on populations that did not include students with sensory or severe physical disabilities.** The norms do not apply. Trying to "just adapt" a test — reading items aloud, skipping items, extending time without principled rationale — invalidates the norms without providing a defensible alternative. The result is a number that cannot be interpreted.

Three domains of knowledge matter for a school psychologist:

**1. Knowing the population.** Low-incidence populations are heterogeneous. A child who is deaf from birth and uses ASL as a first language has fundamentally different assessment needs than a child who became deaf at age 8 after acquiring spoken English. A student with cerebral palsy affecting motor function alone needs different adaptations than one with cerebral palsy plus cognitive impairment. Federal IDEA categories — *Hearing Impairment, Deaf-Blindness, Visual Impairment including Blindness, Orthopedic Impairment, Other Health Impairment, Multiple Disabilities, Traumatic Brain Injury* — provide the classification but not the assessment roadmap.

**2. Knowing the appropriate instruments.** For sensory and severe physical disabilities, assessment typically involves one or more of:

- **Nonverbal cognitive tests** designed for or validated with specific populations: Leiter-3 (designed for use with individuals with hearing impairments, language disorders, or communication difficulties), UNIT-2 (designed as language-reduced), TONI-4. For deaf/hard-of-hearing students, the *WISC-V with the use of a certified ASL interpreter trained in standardized test administration* is one accepted approach; another is a nonverbal-only composite.
- **Adaptive behavior scales** (Vineland-3, ABAS-3) — especially critical for students with multiple or severe disabilities, where cognitive measures alone do not capture functional skills.
- **Functional vision and functional hearing assessments** conducted by a Teacher of the Visually Impaired (TVI) or educational audiologist — not by the school psychologist directly. These are specialty roles.
- **Braille literacy assessments, expanded core curriculum (ECC) evaluations** for students who are blind/visually impaired.

**3. Knowing the team.** Low-incidence assessment is inherently multidisciplinary. The school psychologist is rarely the sole evaluator. The team typically includes a teacher of the deaf/hard of hearing, a teacher of the visually impaired, an orientation and mobility specialist, an occupational therapist, a physical therapist, a speech-language pathologist, and outside medical consultants. The school psychologist contributes cognitive, social-emotional, and behavioral data that the specialists cannot, and integrates findings across disciplines.

A universal principle: **use of translators and interpreters follows the same rules as assessment of English learners**. A certified interpreter trained in standardized testing — not a family member, not an untrained bilingual staff member — must conduct the interpretation. The test report must clearly state that an interpreter was used and describe any limitations this imposes on score interpretation.

## Worked Example

The district school psychologist, Mr. Okafor, is assigned to an initial evaluation for Jaya, an 8-year-old student who is profoundly deaf, uses ASL as her primary language, and has a hearing family that also signs. The district has limited experience with deaf students.

Mr. Okafor's first action is not to schedule testing — it is to convene the right team. He works with:

- A certified **ASL interpreter** trained in standardized test administration.
- A **teacher of the deaf/hard of hearing** who knows Jaya's communication profile.
- An **educational audiologist** to confirm hearing levels and amplification status.
- The **family**, for developmental history and communication preferences.

His cognitive assessment uses the **Leiter-3** (nonverbal, designed for populations with hearing and language differences) rather than attempting the WISC-V without specialist support. He administers the **Vineland-3** via parent and teacher forms to capture adaptive behavior across home and school. The teacher of the deaf administers a signed receptive/expressive language assessment. The audiologist reports current hearing thresholds and amplification status.

Mr. Okafor's integrated report notes that the Leiter-3 yields a Nonverbal IQ of 102 (average range), Vineland-3 composite 85 (low-average), and receptive/expressive ASL within age expectations per the deaf-ed specialist. Importantly, his report **does not** report a Verbal IQ or a Full-Scale IQ — the former is not validly measured in a student whose first language is ASL, and the latter requires a verbal component. Instead, the nonverbal IQ is presented alongside a thorough description of language functioning in ASL.

This report is defensible, useful to the IEP team, and respects the limits of the standardized tools. A report that tried to administer the WISC-V with a casual interpreter and reported a Full-Scale IQ would be indefensible and could actively harm Jaya by mischaracterizing her cognitive functioning.

## Named Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "You can use a standard cognitive test with any student if you extend time."**
Reality: Time extension is a common but insufficient accommodation. It does not address sensory access (deaf students still cannot hear verbal subtests; blind students still cannot see visual stimuli) or motor access (students with severe motor impairments may be unable to point or manipulate). Accommodations that preserve construct validity require specific design — and for some students, an entirely different instrument is the correct choice.

**Misconception 2: "An untrained family member can interpret during testing."**
Reality: Use of family members or untrained interpreters introduces unknown error and compromises standardization. Best practice — and in many states a legal requirement — is a certified interpreter trained in standardized test administration. The report must disclose interpreter use and its implications for score interpretation.

**Misconception 3: "Low-incidence students are rare, so I don't need to know this."**
Reality: The Praxis explicitly tests familiarity with low-incidence assessment (I.A.4.c). Even if you rarely encounter these students, knowing when standard tools fail and who to bring in is the professional baseline. A misadministered assessment on a low-incidence student produces invalid scores that then drive placement and programming decisions — high stakes from a single case.

## If You Only Remember One Thing

**For students with sensory, severe physical, or significant chronic health impairments, the first question is not "Which test do I give?" but "Which tools are valid for this student, and which specialists do I partner with?" Standard cognitive tests often do not apply without specialized adaptation or alternatives.**

## Analogy

A low-incidence assessment is like **a medical workup for a rare condition**: the generalist (school psychologist) leads intake and integration, but specialists (TVI, TOD, audiologist, OT, PT) do the specialized measurement. The generalist's job is not to be the specialist — it is to know which specialists are needed and to integrate their findings into a coherent picture of the student.

## Visual Primitive

**Recommendation: A decision-tree flowchart plus a team-roles diagram.**

- **Decision tree:** Start with "Does the student have a sensory or severe motor impairment that limits access to standard test materials or instructions?" → If YES, branch to population-specific paths (Deaf/HH → Leiter-3, WISC-V with certified ASL interpreter, signed language assessment; Blind/VI → Leiter-3 is not appropriate, use tactile/verbal alternatives, TVI for functional vision + ECC; Severe motor → access-modified tools, AAC-supported assessment, OT/PT consultation). → If NO, proceed with standard battery with appropriate accommodations.

Arrows show data flow inward to integrated report.

## Concept-Specific Mini-Quiz Stems

1. **Stem:** A school psychologist is preparing to evaluate a 10-year-old student who is profoundly deaf and uses ASL as her primary language. Which approach is *most* appropriate?
   **Answer:** Use a language-reduced cognitive instrument (e.g., Leiter-3) and/or the WISC-V administered by the psychologist with a certified ASL interpreter trained in standardized test administration, paired with adaptive behavior and signed-language assessments by appropriate specialists; report a nonverbal composite rather than a full-scale IQ unless verbal subtests are validly accessible in ASL.

2. **Stem:** Why is the use of an untrained bilingual staff member as an interpreter during standardized testing considered poor practice?
   **Answer:** Untrained interpreters introduce unknown error into standardized administration, which compromises the validity of scores and the test norms; best practice requires a certified interpreter trained in standardized test administration, with interpreter use and its limitations disclosed in the report.

3. **Stem:** A school psychologist is leading an assessment for a student with multiple disabilities, including cerebral palsy and a severe hearing impairment. Which of the following is the *most* defensible professional stance?
   **Answer:** Convene a multidisciplinary team (teacher of the deaf/hard of hearing, educational audiologist, occupational/physical therapists, speech-language pathologist) and use an integrated assessment approach that combines adaptive behavior measures, language/communication assessment, and a carefully selected, appropriately accessible cognitive instrument rather than attempting to administer the standard battery alone.
