# MOD-GAP-06: Implementation Science and Change Management

*Closes ETS gap IV.B.4 — "Is familiar with the process involved in implementing individual- and system-level change, including planning and evaluating activities, monitoring fidelity, and addressing barriers to change." Proposed new skill: **RES-04 (Implementation Science and Change Management)**.*

## In Plain Language

Research tells us which interventions work in controlled studies. **Implementation science** is the companion discipline that asks the harder question: why do some of those interventions fail in real schools, while others succeed? It turns out that whether an intervention helps students depends at least as much on *how* it is implemented as on *whether* it is evidence-based. A Tier 1 RCT-supported reading program that is rolled out without teacher training, without leadership support, and without fidelity monitoring will produce no effects. The same program rolled out with attention to implementation drivers will. For a school psychologist, implementation science reframes the job: you are not just an intervention selector; you are an implementation steward.

## First-Principles Explanation

The seminal finding of implementation science (Fixsen and colleagues, 2005; 2013) is simple and hard. If you plot the relationship between "evidence base of the intervention" and "student outcomes," the correlation is surprisingly weak — until you include a third variable: **implementation quality**. Only evidence-based interventions implemented with fidelity produce the expected effects. Everything else — either an unsupported intervention, or a supported intervention poorly implemented — produces little.

Three frameworks dominate the field:

**1. Fixsen's Implementation Stages.** Change unfolds across four stages, each taking months or years:

- **Exploration:** identifying a need, selecting an evidence-based intervention, assessing fit with context.
- **Installation:** preparing the infrastructure — training staff, adjusting policies, gathering materials.
- **Initial Implementation:** early use, where fidelity is fragile and support needs are highest.
- **Full Implementation:** routine use at scale, with fidelity monitoring integrated into regular operations.

Skipping stages is the most common failure mode. Districts often jump from exploration to initial implementation without installation, and initial implementation collapses.

**2. Implementation Drivers.** Three drivers sustain any intervention:

- **Competency drivers** (staff selection, training, coaching, performance assessment) — do the adults have the skills?
- **Organization drivers** (decision-support data systems, facilitative administration, systems intervention) — does the organization support the work?
- **Leadership drivers** (technical leadership, adaptive leadership) — is leadership solving the right problems?

Missing any of the three undermines the others. Training without coaching (competency gap), coaching without leadership buy-in (leadership gap), or leadership support without data systems (organization gap) all predict failure.

**3. Fidelity Monitoring.** **Treatment fidelity** (also called treatment integrity) is the degree to which an intervention is delivered as designed. Fidelity has multiple dimensions: **adherence** (were the components delivered?), **exposure/dosage** (how much? for how long?), **quality** (how well?), **responsiveness** (did students engage?), and **program differentiation** (distinct from other interventions?). Without fidelity data, a null result is uninterpretable — you cannot tell whether the intervention does not work or was not actually delivered.

**Barriers to change** are predictable and can be planned for. Common categories include resource barriers (time, funding, staffing), competency barriers (training, skills), cultural barriers (staff buy-in, community values), technical barriers (systems and data), and leadership barriers (turnover, mixed messages). Effective implementation includes a barriers-and-facilitators assessment *before* initial implementation — not after the rollout stalls.

## Worked Example

Riverside Unified adopts a social-emotional learning program — Second Step — across all elementary schools after an RCT-supported meta-analysis confirmed moderate effects on behavior outcomes. The assistant superintendent distributes the curriculum, holds a single half-day training in August, and announces implementation for September. In January, she reports that the program "isn't working" — incident reports have not decreased.

Dr. Lin, the district school psychologist, is asked to evaluate. Using an implementation-science lens, she conducts an implementation audit instead of re-evaluating the intervention. Her findings:

- **Exploration** was skipped entirely. Teachers had no input on selection; some schools already ran a competing SEL program.
- **Installation** was compressed: materials were distributed but there was no coaching structure, no fidelity measure, and no data system for tracking lesson delivery.
- **Competency drivers:** the half-day training was insufficient; follow-up coaching was absent; no performance feedback loop existed.
- **Fidelity data:** a brief teacher survey suggests the program is being delivered in roughly 40% of classrooms and not at all in others.

Dr. Lin recommends the district pause, revisit exploration and installation, train coaches, build a fidelity checklist, and relaunch with a one-school pilot. The RCT-level evidence base of Second Step is unchanged; what failed was the implementation. The district's "the program doesn't work" conclusion was not supported by data — there was no program actually in place to evaluate.

## Named Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Selecting an evidence-based intervention is the hard part."**
Reality: Selection is the easier half. Evidence registries (What Works Clearinghouse, Evidence for ESSA) make identifying evidence-based interventions relatively straightforward. The hard part is implementation — stages, drivers, fidelity, and barriers. Most failures occur post-selection, not at selection.

**Misconception 2: "If the intervention doesn't show effects after one year, it doesn't work in this school."**
Reality: Fixsen's stages suggest that **2–4 years** of implementation is typically required before full-implementation effects are observable. Premature evaluation is itself a barrier. A null result at 12 months is consistent with early-implementation fidelity problems, not intervention failure. Before abandoning an evidence-based intervention, an implementation audit is essential.

**Misconception 3: "Fidelity is about following a script."**
Reality: Fidelity is about **preserving the active ingredients** of the intervention — the mechanisms of change. Some components of a program are essential (the active ingredients); others are peripheral (formatting, pacing, local examples). Fidelity monitoring focuses on the essential components and allows for thoughtful adaptation elsewhere. This is why fidelity checklists are developed by the program developers, not improvised by implementers.

## If You Only Remember One Thing

**Evidence base + implementation quality = student outcomes. Either variable at zero produces zero. Most "failed" interventions failed on implementation, not on evidence base.**

## Analogy

An evidence-based intervention is like a **prescription medication**. The clinical trial established the medication works *when taken as directed*. In the field, half of patients do not fill the prescription, a quarter stop after a few days, and a quarter take it wrong. When the medication "fails," the problem is almost never pharmacology. It is adherence — implementation. Fidelity monitoring is the adherence check.

## Visual Primitive

**Recommendation: A four-stage progression diagram plus a three-driver triangle.**

- Stage diagram (horizontal): Exploration → Installation → Initial Implementation → Full Implementation. Time axis below (months/years, with the caveat that full implementation typically takes 2–4 years). Each stage has 2–3 key activities listed beneath.
- Implementation-drivers triangle: three vertices labeled Competency (selection, training, coaching, performance assessment), Organization (data systems, facilitative admin, systems intervention), and Leadership (technical + adaptive). Each vertex feeds the others. Inside the triangle: the target — "Sustained use of effective interventions."
- Optional third visual: a 2×2 grid with "Evidence base" on one axis and "Implementation quality" on the other, showing that only the top-right quadrant produces student outcomes.

## Concept-Specific Mini-Quiz Stems

1. **Stem:** A school adopts an evidence-based reading intervention and reports no improvement in student outcomes after the first year. What should the school psychologist do *first*?
   **Answer:** Conduct an implementation audit — assess the stages of implementation completed, the implementation drivers in place (competency, organization, leadership), and fidelity data — before concluding that the intervention is ineffective.

2. **Stem:** Which of the following best describes why fidelity monitoring is essential to interpreting intervention outcomes?
   **Answer:** Without fidelity data, a null result is uninterpretable, because there is no way to distinguish between an ineffective intervention and an intervention that was not actually implemented as designed.

3. **Stem:** A district is preparing to adopt a new behavior-management program. Which implementation activity is *most* appropriate in the exploration stage?
   **Answer:** Assess the fit between the intervention and the local context, including staff buy-in, resource availability, competing programs, and anticipated barriers, before committing to selection.
