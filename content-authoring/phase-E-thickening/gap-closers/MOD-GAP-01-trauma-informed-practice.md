# MOD-GAP-01: Trauma and Trauma-Informed Practice

*Closes ETS gap II.B.1.d — "Knows the impact of trauma on social, emotional, behavioral, and academic functioning; practices to reduce the effects of trauma on learning and behavior." Proposed new skill: **MBH-06 (Trauma-Informed Schools)**.*

## In Plain Language

Trauma is not just something that happened — it is the nervous-system imprint of an event that overwhelmed a child's ability to cope. A student who freezes when the intercom buzzes, or who explodes when a teacher raises their voice, may not be defiant; they may be reliving. Trauma-informed practice is the deliberate shift from asking *"What is wrong with this student?"* to *"What happened to this student?"* For school psychologists, the job is not to do trauma therapy inside school walls — it is to recognize trauma responses, reduce re-traumatization, and connect students to the right tier of support. The building, the schedule, and the adults in it are either healing or harming. There is no neutral.

## First-Principles Explanation

Trauma changes the brain. Repeated activation of the amygdala (threat center) and chronic elevation of cortisol during childhood rewire how the prefrontal cortex — the seat of executive function — develops and regulates behavior. This is why trauma-exposed students often show difficulty with working memory, impulse control, emotional regulation, and sustained attention in ways that look identical to ADHD or ODD but have a different origin. Misdiagnosis is common.

The **Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs)** study (Felitti & Anda, 1998) established the epidemiological foundation: as the number of ACEs rises from 0 to 4+, the risk of learning problems, mental illness, chronic disease, and early death rises in a dose-response pattern. The 10 original ACEs span abuse (physical, emotional, sexual), neglect (physical, emotional), and household dysfunction (parental divorce, domestic violence, parental mental illness, parental substance use, parental incarceration). Modern framings add community-level ACEs (racism, poverty, community violence, forced migration).

Trauma-informed schools operate on **SAMHSA's four R's**: Realize the widespread impact of trauma; Recognize signs and symptoms; Respond by integrating knowledge into policies and practice; and Resist re-traumatization. This is a universal (Tier 1) orientation — every adult, every classroom — layered with Tier 2 small-group supports (e.g., Bounce Back, CBITS) and Tier 3 clinical interventions (e.g., TF-CBT delivered by a mental health clinician, often outside the school).

The test-winning distinction: trauma-informed is a **school-wide stance**, not a program you buy. TF-CBT is a **clinical treatment**, not something a teacher delivers.

## Worked Example

Marcus is a 9-year-old who came to Jefferson Elementary in October after his mother lost housing. He is frequently late, falls asleep in class, and lashes out physically when peers bump into him in line. His classroom teacher refers him for a behavior plan. The principal considers suspension for the third physical incident.

Ms. Rivera, the school psychologist, reframes the case: she reviews Marcus's cumulative file (prior school reported no behavior concerns), speaks with his mother (the family is in a shelter; Marcus witnesses adult conflict nightly), and observes Marcus in class. The observation reveals that he startles at the PA system, has no reliable eating schedule, and his outbursts follow physical contact from peers.

Her recommendations are tiered. Tier 1: the school adopts predictable transitions, warns before the PA comes on, and keeps breakfast available until 9:30. Tier 2: Marcus joins a 10-week Bounce Back small group run by the school counselor. Tier 3: Ms. Rivera refers the family to a community mental health center for TF-CBT and helps them enroll.

Six weeks in, Marcus's incident reports drop from three per week to zero. He has not been "fixed" — his environment has been made safer, his body has started to feel predictable, and a clinician is doing the trauma-focused work outside school hours.

## Named Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Trauma-informed means every teacher should provide trauma therapy."**
Reality: Trauma-informed means every adult recognizes trauma responses and avoids re-traumatizing students. Trauma *therapy* (TF-CBT, EMDR, CPP) requires licensure and specialized training and usually happens outside school or via a contracted clinician. School psychologists typically screen, consult, refer, and support — they do not do weekly trauma therapy during the school day unless they are specifically trained, supervised, and authorized.

**Misconception 2: "If a student has trauma, they need a Tier 3 intervention."**
Reality: Most students with trauma exposure are best served by Tier 1 (predictable, regulated environments) and Tier 2 (small-group skill-building like Bounce Back, CBITS, SPARCS). Tier 3 is reserved for students whose symptoms are acute, persistent, and functionally impairing despite lower-tier supports. Jumping straight to Tier 3 misses the point of the MTSS framework.

**Misconception 3: "ACEs scores predict individual outcomes."**
Reality: ACEs is an epidemiological tool designed for population-level risk analysis. An individual student with 5 ACEs may be thriving; another with 1 ACE may be in crisis. Use ACEs to understand population risk and plan system-level supports, not to label individual students. Scoring a child's ACEs and sharing the number with teachers is inappropriate without a clear clinical purpose.

## If You Only Remember One Thing

**Trauma-informed = every adult, every classroom, every day. Trauma therapy = a trained clinician in a confidential setting. Confusing the two is the most common test trap.**

## Analogy

Think of a trauma-informed school as a **smoke-free building**, not a **smoking-cessation clinic**. The building policy protects everyone from harm by design. The clinic is where people who are actively struggling get specialized help. Schools must be the building. A small number of clinicians inside or outside the building provide the clinic.

## Visual Primitive

**Recommendation: Three-tier pyramid overlaid on a "4 R's" circular diagram.**

- Pyramid base (Tier 1, 80% of students): Universal practices — predictable routines, warm greetings, regulation breaks, warning before transitions, safe spaces.
- Pyramid middle (Tier 2, 15%): Small-group evidence-based programs — CBITS, Bounce Back, SPARCS.
- Pyramid top (Tier 3, 5%): Individual clinical treatment — TF-CBT, EMDR, CPP, typically delivered by a licensed mental health clinician.
- The 4 R's (Realize, Recognize, Respond, Resist re-traumatization) encircle the pyramid, showing that all tiers require the trauma-informed orientation.

A secondary visual: a 2x2 matrix contrasting "Behavior response" vs. "Trauma response" on observable behaviors (tantrum, refusal, shutdown, aggression) — highlighting why the same surface behavior needs different interpretations.

## Concept-Specific Mini-Quiz Stems

1. **Stem:** A 2nd-grade teacher reports that a student becomes aggressive whenever a peer brushes against her in line. Records show the student recently transitioned from a domestic violence shelter. What is the most appropriate *first* response for the school psychologist?
   **Answer:** Consult with the teacher to modify the classroom environment (e.g., give the student space at the front of the line, provide warnings before transitions) while referring the family to a community clinician for TF-CBT evaluation.

2. **Stem:** Which of the following best describes a trauma-informed school?
   **Answer:** Every adult in the building has been trained to recognize trauma responses and avoid practices that re-traumatize students, regardless of whether individual students have disclosed trauma histories.

3. **Stem:** A principal proposes administering the ACEs questionnaire to every incoming 6th grader and flagging students with scores of 4 or higher to teachers. What is the most appropriate response from the school psychologist?
   **Answer:** Decline the proposal. ACEs was designed as a population-level epidemiological tool, not an individual screening or labeling instrument. Universal screening using an adapted SEL screener with a trauma-sensitive subscale, followed by a clinically appropriate process for flagged students, would be more defensible.
