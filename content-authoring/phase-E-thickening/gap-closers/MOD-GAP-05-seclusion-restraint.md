# MOD-GAP-05: Seclusion, Restraint, and Manifestation Determination

*Closes ETS gap IV.C.2.c — "Knows the rights of students (e.g., informed consent, confidentiality, least restrictive environment, **manifestation determination, seclusion and restraint**)." Both seclusion/restraint and manifestation determination are named explicitly by ETS but lacked dedicated coverage. Proposed new skill / sub-concept: **LEG-06 (Seclusion, Restraint, Manifestation Determination)** or sub-concept under LEG-02.*

## In Plain Language

This module is about the edge cases of special education law — the moments when a student's rights are most vulnerable and when professional judgment matters most. **Seclusion and restraint** are the most restrictive interventions a school can legally use, and they are governed by a patchwork of state laws because there is no single federal statute. **Manifestation determination** is the meeting that asks a specific yes/no question: *was the student's conduct a direct result of their disability?* The answer changes everything — including whether the student can be disciplined the same way as a peer without a disability. School psychologists are central to both conversations because both turn on data that only a psychologist can reliably gather and interpret.

## First-Principles Explanation

**Seclusion** is the involuntary confinement of a student alone in a room or area that the student is prevented from leaving. **Restraint** is the use of physical, mechanical, or chemical means to restrict a student's movement. Federal law (IDEA, Section 504, and the ADA) does not explicitly authorize or prohibit seclusion and restraint in most situations, but the U.S. Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights and the Office of Special Education and Rehabilitative Services have issued guidance consistently holding that:

- Seclusion and restraint should be used **only in emergencies where there is imminent danger of serious physical harm** to the student or others.
- They should **never be used as discipline, convenience, or for noncompliance**.
- Less restrictive alternatives must have been attempted or considered.
- Every incident must be documented, reviewed, and reported to parents, usually within 24 hours.
- Students must not be denied FAPE because of how seclusion or restraint is used.

State laws add specific requirements — many prohibit prone restraint, require staff training, set duration limits, and mandate incident reporting. The Praxis tests the underlying principles, not a specific state's statute.

**Manifestation determination** is an IDEA procedural safeguard (34 CFR §300.530(e)). When a school proposes a disciplinary change of placement for more than 10 consecutive school days — or a pattern of removals totaling more than 10 cumulative school days that constitutes a change of placement — an IEP team must convene within 10 school days of the decision and answer two questions:

1. Was the conduct **caused by, or had a direct and substantial relationship to, the student's disability**?
2. Was the conduct the **direct result of the school's failure to implement the IEP**?

If the answer to either is YES, the behavior is a manifestation of the disability. Consequences:

- The student cannot be disciplined in the same manner as a non-disabled peer.
- The IEP team must conduct an FBA (if one has not been completed) and implement a behavior intervention plan (BIP), or review and modify an existing BIP.
- The student generally returns to the placement from which they were removed, unless the parent and school agree otherwise or a specific special circumstance applies (weapons, drugs, serious bodily injury — the "special circumstances" exception allowing up to 45 school days in an Interim Alternative Educational Setting).

If the answer to both is NO, the student can be disciplined the same way as a peer without a disability — but the school must still provide FAPE during any removal longer than 10 school days, usually in an Interim Alternative Educational Setting.

The test-winning distinction: **the manifestation determination is about causation, not severity**. A student's behavior can be egregious and still be a manifestation of disability. It can be mild and not a manifestation. The question is causation.

## Worked Example

Lucas is a 7th grader with an IEP under the Other Health Impairment category (ADHD, combined presentation). He has been suspended five times this school year, totaling 11 cumulative school days. After a fight in the cafeteria, the administration proposes a 10-day suspension that will push his total to 21 school days — a change of placement. An IEP team convenes for a manifestation determination within 10 school days.

Dr. Rivera, the school psychologist, presents data: Lucas's IEP includes a behavior goal targeting impulsive physical responses, which the team established based on his ADHD diagnosis and prior FBA. Classroom observations show that when Lucas is in unstructured settings (cafeteria, hallway, transitions), he has difficulty inhibiting impulsive physical responses — documented in the FBA as a function of his disability. The cafeteria fight fits this pattern: a peer bumped him, and Lucas responded with a shove before he was able to access the coping strategies listed in his BIP.

The team answers the two questions:
1. Was the conduct caused by, or have a direct and substantial relationship to, the disability? **Yes** — the FBA data show the behavior is a direct manifestation of his ADHD-related impulse control deficits.
2. Was the conduct the direct result of the school's failure to implement the IEP? **Partially** — the cafeteria is an unstructured setting and the IEP does not include a plan for supervision during unstructured times.

Because at least one answer is yes, Lucas's conduct is a manifestation. The team cannot impose the disciplinary removal beyond 10 days. The IEP is amended to include cafeteria supervision accommodations, the BIP is updated, and Lucas returns to his placement. The administration is reminded that a subsequent similar incident requires IEP team review before any additional removal.

Note what this case is **not**: it is not a question of whether Lucas "should be held accountable." Under IDEA, students with disabilities remain accountable — the question is whether the form of accountability is consistent with the student's disability.

## Named Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Seclusion can be used when a student refuses to follow directions."**
Reality: Federal guidance and most state laws reserve seclusion (and restraint) for situations of **imminent danger of serious physical harm**. Refusal to follow directions, insubordination, disruption, or profanity are not legitimate grounds. Using seclusion or restraint for discipline or convenience exposes the school to civil rights violations and potential Section 504 / IDEA / ADA complaints.

**Misconception 2: "A manifestation determination is a judgment about the severity of the student's behavior."**
Reality: The legal question is about **causation** — whether the behavior was caused by, or had a direct and substantial relationship to, the disability — not whether the behavior was severe. A student can commit a very serious act that is a manifestation of disability, and a student can commit a mild act that is not. The severity of the behavior does not answer the manifestation question.

**Misconception 3: "If the behavior is a manifestation, the student receives no consequence."**
Reality: If the behavior is a manifestation, the student cannot be disciplined the same way as a non-disabled peer, and the IEP/BIP must be reviewed or developed. However, the student may still be subject to consequences that are consistent with their disability (e.g., therapeutic response, BIP-driven consequences, therapeutic removal short of a change of placement). Also, the special circumstances exception allows placement in an Interim Alternative Educational Setting for up to 45 school days for weapons, drugs, or serious bodily injury — regardless of manifestation determination.

## If You Only Remember One Thing

**Seclusion/restraint: emergency-only, last-resort, documented. Manifestation determination: a causation question, not a severity question — yes to either prong means the student cannot be disciplined as a non-disabled peer and the IEP team must act.**

## Analogy

Think of the **manifestation determination** as a **medical/legal question in one meeting**: two doctors (the IEP team) are asked whether a specific symptom (the behavior) is caused by a diagnosed condition (the disability). The answer determines the treatment plan (BIP, placement), not the moral status of the patient.

## Visual Primitive

**Recommendation: Two flowcharts side-by-side.**

- **Flowchart A (Seclusion/Restraint decision path):** Emergency? → Imminent serious physical harm? → Less restrictive alternatives attempted/considered? → If all YES, may proceed; else STOP. Each "STOP" branch lists required steps (de-escalation, behavioral supports, parental notification).
- **Flowchart B (Manifestation determination):** Proposed removal >10 school days? → Convene IEP team within 10 school days → Ask Prong 1 (causation) → Ask Prong 2 (IEP implementation) → If either YES, behavior is a manifestation (return to placement, review/develop BIP, conduct FBA if needed) → If both NO, same discipline as non-disabled peer (but FAPE continues for removal >10 days).

## Concept-Specific Mini-Quiz Stems

1. **Stem:** Which of the following is the *most appropriate* use of physical restraint in a school setting?
   **Answer:** A student is in imminent danger of serious physical harm to self or others, de-escalation strategies have failed, and trained staff apply the minimum restraint necessary for the minimum duration, followed by documentation and parental notification.

2. **Stem:** A student with an IEP for Specific Learning Disability is proposed for an 11th consecutive day of disciplinary removal after a fight. An IEP team is convened for a manifestation determination. Which question *must* the team consider?
   **Answer:** Whether the conduct was caused by, or had a direct and substantial relationship to, the student's disability — and whether the conduct was the direct result of the school's failure to implement the IEP.

3. **Stem:** A manifestation determination team concludes that the student's behavior was a manifestation of disability. Which of the following is the IEP team *required* to do?
   **Answer:** Conduct a functional behavioral assessment (if one has not already been conducted) and implement a behavior intervention plan, or review and modify an existing BIP; the student generally returns to the placement from which they were removed.
