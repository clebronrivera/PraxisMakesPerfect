# MOD-D1-09: Curriculum-Based Measurement (CBM) — Progress Tracking, Not Replacement

## In Plain Language

A Curriculum-Based Measurement (CBM) is a quick, standardized test that comes straight from a student's own classroom curriculum — not a nationally normed test. A teacher gives a student a 2-minute reading fluency probe using a passage from the class reading book, or a 5-minute math computation probe using problems from the math series. The student's performance on that probe is compared to classmates doing the same work. CBMs are collected frequently (weekly, biweekly) to track whether a student is making progress during an intervention. They are not designed to replace standardized tests like the WISC or the Woodcock-Johnson. Instead, they supplement standardized testing by providing quick, frequent snapshots of progress in the actual curriculum the student is using.

## First-Principles Explanation

CBM grew out of the RTI movement and represents a practical approach to frequent progress monitoring. Unlike standardized tests (which take hours, cost money, and are given once or twice a year), CBMs are:

- **Brief:** 2–5 minutes per student
- **Low-cost:** Often teacher-created or from commercially available probe banks
- **Frequent:** Can be given weekly without burden
- **Curriculum-derived:** Uses actual materials from the student's classroom curriculum, not external passages
- **Locally norm-referenced:** Compared to classmates or grade-level peers, not national norms
- **Sensitive to growth:** Designed to detect small changes in performance over weeks and months, not just yearly snapshots

**Key distinction: CBM vs. CBA**

The literature sometimes conflates these terms, so clarity is important:

1. **Curriculum-Based Assessment (CBA)** is the broader umbrella. It means any assessment derived from the curriculum, which could include teacher-made tests, work samples, portfolios, structured observations, and CBMs. CBA is not a specific assessment method; it is a category.

2. **Curriculum-Based Measurement (CBM)** is a specific type of CBA with standardized administration, scoring, and interpretation. A CBM reading probe has a specific format (1-minute or 2-minute oral reading fluency passage), specific scoring rules (words read correctly per minute), and specific interpretation (compared to local norms). A CBM in math computation might be a specific set of problems administered for 3 minutes, scored by counting correct digits per minute, compared to grade-level benchmarks.

The difference matters on the Praxis exam. If a question asks, "What is the most appropriate assessment for weekly progress monitoring during a reading intervention?" the answer is CBM (frequent, sensitive, curriculum-based). If the question asks, "How should a school psychologist assess a student's competence in the curriculum?" the answer might be CBA (broader category, which includes many methods).

**Standard CBM metrics:**

- **Oral Reading Fluency (ORF):** Words read correctly per minute from a grade-level passage
- **Math Computation (MC):** Digits written correctly per minute on a computation probe
- **Spelling (SP):** Letter sequences written correctly per minute
- **Phonological Awareness:** Sound tasks (rhyming, blending, segmenting) with accuracy and speed
- **Handwriting (HW):** Letters written correctly per minute

Each metric has **benchmarks** — expected performance at different points in the year. For example, a second grader in January should read approximately 50–60 words correct per minute on a second-grade passage. If the student is reading 35 WCM, they are below benchmark and may need intervention.

**Relationship to standardized testing:**

CBM is NOT a replacement for standardized cognitive or achievement testing. Here is the relationship:

1. **Standardized test (e.g., WISC, Woodcock-Johnson):** Norm-referenced, comprehensive, expensive, once or twice per year. Answers the question: "How does this student compare to national peers overall?"

2. **CBM:** Curriculum-based, frequent, local-norm referenced, inexpensive, weekly. Answers the question: "Is this student progressing in the actual curriculum they are using?"

A school might use a standardized achievement test in fall to identify at-risk students, then use CBM weekly during intervention to monitor progress, then give another standardized test in spring to see if intervention closed the gap. Both are necessary for complete data.

## Worked Example

Lincoln Elementary uses RTI for reading. The school has adopted a benchmark of 60 words correct per minute (WCM) by the end of second grade.

**Fall (September) Identification:**
- Universal screening using CBM ORF. All second graders read a 2-minute passage from their reading series. Results show that 24% of the class is below the expected benchmark of 40 WCM (end-of-summer goal for starting second grade).

**Intervention Decision:**
- Those 24% (approximately 6 students) are placed in a small-group reading intervention (Tier 2). They continue core instruction (Tier 1) plus 4 days per week of a targeted fluency intervention.

**Progress Monitoring (Weekly):**
- Every Tuesday, each student in the intervention group reads a CBM passage (a different passage each week, but all passages are from the same second-grade reading series). Fluency is measured in WCM. The data is graphed.

Example data for one student (Marcus):
- Week 1 (Sept. 17): 28 WCM
- Week 2 (Sept. 24): 31 WCM
- Week 3 (Oct. 1): 33 WCM
- Week 4 (Oct. 8): 35 WCM
- Week 5 (Oct. 15): 35 WCM
- Week 6 (Oct. 22): 36 WCM

**Interpretation:** Marcus is making slow but steady progress. The trend line shows improvement (28 to 36 in 6 weeks = 1.3 WCM gain per week). However, at this rate, Marcus will reach the end-of-year benchmark (60 WCM) in about 18 weeks, which is feasible. The intervention is working, though it is not miraculous. The team monitors further and may increase intervention intensity if progress stalls.

**Spring Outcome:**
- In January, Marcus has reached 50 WCM. Benchmark expectation for mid-year is 55 WCM, so Marcus is close. He is exited from Tier 2 and continues Tier 1 with enhanced monitoring.
- In May (spring standardized testing), Marcus's Woodcock-Johnson Reading Fluency score is 92 (low-average, better than his fall score but still below average). The CBM data showed progress and predicted this outcome.

**Comparison to standardized test alone:**
- If the school had only given a standardized test in fall and spring, they would have known Marcus was behind in September, could see he improved by May, but would NOT have known whether the intervention was working, at what rate, or whether to adjust. The weekly CBM data provided that crucial feedback.

**Example: What is NOT a CBM:**
- A teacher-created spelling test from weekly spelling words — this is a CBA (curriculum-based assessment) but not a CBM (it is not standardized in administration and scoring across classrooms).
- A nationally normed reading test given in fall and spring — this is a standardized assessment, not a CBM (not curriculum-derived or locally normed).
- A work sample portfolio of a student's math problem-solving — this is a CBA but not a CBM (it is not a timed, standardized probe with specific metrics).

## Named Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "CBM and CBA are the same thing."**
Reality: CBA is the umbrella category (any assessment from the curriculum). CBM is a specific type of CBA with standardized administration, scoring, and interpretation. All CBMs are CBAs, but not all CBAs are CBMs.

**Misconception 2: "A CBM can replace a standardized achievement test for identification and eligibility."**
Reality: CBMs are local-norm referenced; standardized tests are national-norm referenced. CBMs are sensitive to progress within the curriculum but do not provide the comprehensive profile that standardized tests (like the Woodcock-Johnson) do. Both are needed for different purposes.

**Misconception 3: "If a student is making progress on CBM, they do not need special education."**
Reality: It depends on the rate of progress. A student could be making progress on CBM (moving from 20 to 25 WCM in reading) but still falling farther behind grade-level peers (who are moving from 50 to 65 WCM). Absolute progress is good; relative progress (closing the gap) is what matters for eligibility decisions.

**Misconception 4: "CBM is only for reading."**
Reality: CBM is used in reading, math, spelling, writing, and phonological awareness. Any domain where you can create a brief, timed probe with clear scoring rules can have a CBM.

**Misconception 5: "Monthly CBM collection is sufficient for progress monitoring."**
Reality: The power of CBM is frequent collection (weekly or biweekly) to detect trends. Monthly is too infrequent to provide useful trend data and adjust interventions quickly.

## If You Only Remember One Thing

**CBM is curriculum-based, frequent, locally norm-referenced progress monitoring. It supplements (not replaces) standardized testing and is essential for RTI Tier 2/3 progress tracking.**

## Analogy

**CBM is the practice drill a baseball team does every day; standardized testing is the season league game.** You need both to know how the team is developing and how they compare to others.

## Concept-Specific Mini-Quiz Stems

1. **Stem:** A teacher wants to monitor a student's math progress weekly during a math intervention. Which assessment is most appropriate?
   **Correct answer:** CBM (Curriculum-Based Measurement). It is brief, frequent, curriculum-based, and designed for weekly progress monitoring.

2. **Stem:** A school psychologist is identifying students who are at-risk in reading. Should they use CBM as the primary tool?
   **Correct answer:** No. CBM is best for progress monitoring in students already receiving intervention. For initial identification across a whole grade, a standardized universal screener (either CBM used school-wide or a standardized screener like DIBELS) is more appropriate.

3. **Stem:** A student's CBM math computation score improves from 15 digits correct per minute in September to 22 digits correct per minute in November, but the class benchmark for November is 35 digits. What does this indicate?
   **Correct answer:** The student is making progress (improved 7 digits in 2 months), but the progress is not sufficient to close the gap with grade-level peers. The intervention may need to be intensified or the student may need a different approach.
