# MOD-D1-06: MTSS Tiers — What Belongs at Tier 1 vs. Tier 3

## In Plain Language

The Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS) is a pyramid with three levels, and each level has completely different characteristics. Tier 1 is the foundation: universal, preventive practices that apply to every single student in the school. Think of clear behavior expectations posted everywhere, direct instruction in the core curriculum, consistent praise when students follow the rules. Everyone gets Tier 1. Tier 3 is the narrow peak: intensive, individualized interventions for the small number of students who are not responding to Tier 1 and Tier 2. The Praxis exam loves to test whether you can distinguish "this belongs in Tier 1 because it's universal" from "this belongs in Tier 3 because it's intensive and individualized." If you see the word "intensive" or "specific students," you are looking at Tier 3.

## First-Principles Explanation

MTSS is a prevention science framework. The logic is public health: identify the biggest levers that benefit the most students, implement them well, then add additional support layers for students who do not respond to the core system.

**Tier 1 (Universal):** All students, all the time.
- Emphasis on prevention and efficiency. A school cannot afford to custom-build a system for every student.
- Features include:
  - **Clear expectations.** The school establishes 3–5 behavioral or academic expectations (e.g., PBIS: "Be Safe, Be Respectful, Be Responsible") and teaches them explicitly to every student.
  - **Explicit, direct instruction.** Tier 1 is the core curriculum. For reading, it is the reading series used for all students. For math, it is the math curriculum. These are research-based and implemented with fidelity.
  - **Universal monitoring.** All students are screened for academic and behavioral risk (universal screening in RTI).
  - **Corrective feedback.** When a student violates an expectation, the feedback is immediate and consistent. This is available to all.
  - **Positive reinforcement.** The school recognizes students who meet expectations, making prosocial behavior visible and rewarded.
- Tier 1 affects 80% of students. Most students respond to good Tier 1 instruction and clear expectations.

**Tier 2 (Targeted/Supplemental):** Students showing early risk, typically 15% of the population.
- These are students who did not respond adequately to Tier 1. They need something more. Examples: small-group reading intervention, check-in/check-out behavior system, a modified homework expectation.
- Tier 2 is still not intensely individualized, but it is targeted to a specific skill or behavior need.
- Delivered to small groups of students with similar needs, not the whole school.

**Tier 3 (Intensive/Specialized):** Students with significant, persistent needs, typically 5% of the population.
- These students require **individualized** interventions designed specifically for their profile. Examples: specialized speech-language therapy, one-on-one tutoring in a specific foundational skill, intensive behavioral intervention with frequent progress monitoring and data-based adjustments.
- Delivered to individual students or very small groups (2–3 students, not 10).
- Requires frequent progress monitoring (weekly or biweekly), not monthly.
- May require outside expertise (speech-language pathologist, Board Certified Behavior Analyst, special educator).

The critical distinction for the exam: **Tier 1 is universal and preventive; Tier 3 is intensive and individualized.** If an answer choice says "provided to all students," it is likely Tier 1. If it says "for students identified as at-risk" or "intensive and frequent," it is likely Tier 3.

## Worked Example

Lincoln Elementary implements MTSS in reading:

**Tier 1 (Universal):**
- All students use the same core reading curriculum (Wilson Fundations for K–2, Core Knowledge for 3–5).
- All students receive 90 minutes of reading instruction daily with explicit phonics, fluency, comprehension.
- All students take a universal screening probe in September, January, and May (ORF, letter-sound knowledge, etc.).
- All students participate in classroom-wide reading activities and book clubs.

In January, universal screening identifies that 22% of second graders are below benchmark in fluency.

**Tier 2 (Targeted):**
- Those 22% of second graders (12 students) are placed in a small-group intervention: "fluency boost" groups of 3–4 students, 20 minutes per day, 4 days per week, focused on repeated reading of familiar passages and automaticity.
- Intervention is delivered alongside core Tier 1 instruction (so the student still gets Tier 1, plus the Tier 2 supplement).
- Progress is monitored monthly to see if the group is making gains.

By April, 9 of the 12 students have made sufficient progress and returned to Tier 1 only. Three students are still struggling.

**Tier 3 (Intensive):**
- Those 3 remaining students (out of the original 22, now 2% of the grade) are placed in intensive individual or 1-on-1 intervention.
- Each receives a specialized fluency intervention targeted to their specific deficit (e.g., one student lacks phonological awareness, one has weak decoding, one has processing speed issues).
- Interventions are different for each student because the underlying skill gap is different.
- Progress is monitored weekly.
- A reading specialist or special educator leads the intervention, not the classroom teacher.

**Non-example: What does NOT belong at Tier 1?**
- "Intensive behavior tracking for students with frequent incidents" — this is Tier 3 (individualized).
- "Teaching social skills to students identified as socially isolated" — depends on context, but if it is in a small group with explicit targeting, it is Tier 2; if it is universal social skills instruction for all students, it could be Tier 1.
- "Professional development for teachers on advanced literacy strategies" — this supports Tier 1 implementation but is not itself a student-facing practice.

**Non-example: What does NOT belong at Tier 3?**
- "Explicit phonics instruction, delivered to all students" — this is Tier 1 (universal).
- "Small-group intervention for students scoring in the 16th–25th percentile" — this is Tier 2, not intensive enough to be Tier 3.

## Named Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Tier 1 is just a baseline; real interventions happen at Tiers 2 and 3."**
Reality: Tier 1 IS an intervention. A high-quality Tier 1 (explicit instruction, clear expectations, positive feedback) prevents the need for Tiers 2 and 3 for most students. Schools with weak Tier 1 struggle with Tier 3 because they are trying to fix downstream what went wrong upstream.

**Misconception 2: "Special education is the same as Tier 3."**
Reality: Special education and Tier 3 are related but distinct. Tier 3 is an intensity level within the general education RTI/MTSS system. Some students in Tier 3 may eventually qualify for special education if they meet IDEA criteria. But Tier 3 is not automatically special education.

**Misconception 3: "If a student is in Tier 2, they no longer receive Tier 1."**
Reality: MTSS is cumulative. A student in Tier 2 still receives all of Tier 1 (core instruction, universal expectations) plus the Tier 2 supplement. Tiers are additive, not mutually exclusive.

**Misconception 4: "The word 'intensive' always means Tier 3."**
Reality: "Intensive" typically implies Tier 3, but context matters. A teacher-delivered intensive block of phonics for a small group might still be Tier 2 if the group includes several students with similar needs and the intervention is still somewhat standardized. True Tier 3 is individualized and tailored to one student's specific profile.

## If You Only Remember One Thing

**Tier 1 = universal (all students). Tier 3 = intensive and individualized (specific students). If you see "all students," think Tier 1. If you see "specific students" or "individualized," think Tier 3.**

## Analogy

**MTSS is like a hospital's triage system.** Tier 1 is the public health campaign (eat well, exercise). Tier 2 is the clinic (a patient with hypertension gets a medication and diet plan). Tier 3 is the ICU (a patient with septic shock gets hourly monitoring, multiple medications, and individualized life support).

## Concept-Specific Mini-Quiz Stems

1. **Stem:** A school implements a school-wide behavior expectation system where all students learn and are reinforced for specific behaviors (being safe, respectful, responsible). Which MTSS tier does this represent?
   **Correct answer:** Tier 1 (universal). It applies to all students, is preventive in nature, and is a core feature of all-school support.

2. **Stem:** A student struggling with reading is placed in a small-group intervention with 4 other students who are also below-grade-level in fluency. The group meets daily for 20 minutes, running a 6-week protocol. This is best classified as:
   **Correct answer:** Tier 2 (targeted). Multiple students with similar needs, small group (not 1-on-1), standard protocol (fluency intervention), duration-limited (6 weeks). If it were truly individualized and ongoing, it would be Tier 3.

3. **Stem:** A student with significant behavioral needs has a one-on-one behavioral intervention plan, including daily check-in with a behavioral coach, individualized token economy, and weekly progress monitoring. This is best classified as:
   **Correct answer:** Tier 3 (intensive). It is individualized to one student, involves specialized personnel, and includes frequent monitoring and adjustment.
