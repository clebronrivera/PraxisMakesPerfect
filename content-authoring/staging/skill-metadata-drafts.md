## Domain II — Student-Level Services

### MBH-06 — Trauma-Informed Educational Practice

**Source module:** `MOD-GAP-01-trauma-informed-practice.md`
**Proposed metadata ID:** `NEW-2-TraumaInformedPractice` (or `MBH-S06` if numbered).

#### Vocabulary

- **Trauma** — The nervous-system imprint of an event that overwhelmed a child's ability to cope; distinct from the event itself.
- **Trauma-informed practice** — A systems stance in which every adult recognizes trauma responses and avoids re-traumatizing students, regardless of individual disclosure.
- **Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs)** — An epidemiological framework (Felitti & Anda, 1998) cataloging abuse, neglect, and household dysfunction with dose-response links to later health and learning outcomes.
- **Original 10 ACEs** — Physical/emotional/sexual abuse; physical/emotional neglect; parental divorce, domestic violence, parental mental illness, parental substance use, parental incarceration.
- **Community-level ACEs** — Modern extensions including racism, poverty, community violence, and forced migration.
- **Toxic stress** — Chronic, unbuffered activation of the stress response during childhood that rewires brain architecture.
- **Cortisol** — Primary stress hormone; chronically elevated levels during childhood disrupt prefrontal-cortex development.
- **Amygdala** — Limbic threat-detection structure; over-activated in trauma-exposed students.
- **Prefrontal cortex (PFC)** — Seat of executive function, working memory, and regulation; development is blunted by chronic cortisol exposure.
- **SAMHSA 4 R's** — Realize, Recognize, Respond, Resist re-traumatization.
- **Re-traumatization** — A subsequent event or systemic response that replicates the original trauma's impact (e.g., punitive discipline triggering a freeze response).
- **Window of tolerance** — The physiological band within which a student can access learning; outside the window the student is hyper-aroused (fight/flight) or hypo-aroused (freeze/shutdown).
- **Fight / Flight / Freeze / Fawn** — Four autonomic trauma responses; outward behavior often misread as defiance.
- **Tier 1 trauma-informed practices** — Universal classroom/school supports (predictable routines, warm greetings, transition warnings, safe spaces).
- **Tier 2 trauma interventions** — Small-group evidence-based programs (CBITS, Bounce Back, SPARCS).
- **Tier 3 trauma treatment** — Individual clinical therapy (TF-CBT, EMDR, CPP) by a licensed clinician.
- **TF-CBT (Trauma-Focused Cognitive Behavioral Therapy)** — Evidence-based trauma treatment; requires licensed clinician; not typically delivered in school by school psychologists.
- **CBITS (Cognitive Behavioral Intervention for Trauma in Schools)** — School-based Tier 2 small-group trauma intervention.
- **Bounce Back** — Elementary-school adaptation of CBITS.
- **SPARCS (Structured Psychotherapy for Adolescents Responding to Chronic Stress)** — Adolescent group trauma intervention.
- **EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing)** — Tier 3 clinical trauma treatment.
- **CPP (Child-Parent Psychotherapy)** — Tier 3 clinical treatment for young children and caregivers.
- **Trauma screening** — Systematic identification (often via adapted SEL screener with trauma-sensitive items), distinct from individual ACEs scoring.
- **Dose-response relationship** — Epidemiological finding that ACE count correlates with outcome severity at the population level — not an individual predictor.

#### Misconceptions

- Believing trauma-informed means every teacher should provide trauma therapy, when in fact it means every adult recognizes trauma responses and avoids re-traumatization — while clinical treatment is delivered by licensed clinicians.
- Believing any student with a trauma history needs a Tier 3 intervention, when in fact most are best served by Tier 1 (predictable environments) and Tier 2 (small-group skill-building); Tier 3 is reserved for acute, persistent, functionally-impairing symptoms despite lower-tier support.
- Believing an individual's ACEs score predicts their individual outcomes, when in fact ACEs is a population-level epidemiological tool; a student with 5 ACEs may thrive while a student with 1 may be in crisis.
- Believing trauma-exposed students' attention and regulation problems reflect ADHD or ODD, when in fact the behavioral profile can be indistinguishable at the surface despite a different etiology — misdiagnosis is common.
- Believing universal ACEs screening is appropriate school practice, when in fact scoring and flagging individuals with ACEs questionnaires is inappropriate without a clinical purpose; adapted SEL screeners with trauma-sensitive subscales are more defensible.

#### Case archetypes

- **Recent-displacement case (Marcus archetype):** Student from unstable housing presents with tardiness, fatigue, and aggression to physical contact. Reframe from discipline to trauma response; layer Tier 1 environmental modifications (transition warnings, extended breakfast), Tier 2 group (Bounce Back), and Tier 3 referral to community clinician for TF-CBT.
- **Suspension-consideration case:** Principal considers removal after a physical incident by a trauma-exposed student. School psychologist redirects toward MTSS and cumulative-file review rather than exclusionary discipline.
- **Universal-screening-proposal case:** Administrator proposes giving the ACEs questionnaire to every incoming student and flagging scores of 4+. School psychologist declines, explains population-vs-individual distinction, proposes adapted SEL screener instead.
- **Surface-behavior-lookalike case:** Student presents with attention and regulation difficulties that match ADHD criteria; history reveals toxic stress exposure. Differential consideration drives intervention planning.

#### Laws / Frameworks

- **SAMHSA's "Four R's" framework** — Realize, Recognize, Respond, Resist re-traumatization. The orienting framework for trauma-informed systems.
- **Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs) Study** — Felitti & Anda (1998); foundational CDC-Kaiser epidemiological study.
- **MTSS / tiered framework** — Applied to trauma: Tier 1 universal, Tier 2 selective, Tier 3 indicated.
- **NASP Position Statement on School-Based Mental Health Services** — Affirms trauma-informed practice as core to school psychology.
- **ESSA school climate provisions** — Provide federal authority for school climate and mental-health work that supports trauma-informed implementation.
- **IDEA — Emotional Disturbance category** — Provides eligibility pathway for some trauma-exposed students but is not itself a trauma framework.

### ACA-10 — Classroom Organization and Management

**Source module:** `MOD-GAP-02-classroom-management.md`
**Proposed metadata ID:** `NEW-2-ClassroomManagement`
**Note:** Per the source module's metadata, the more accurate top-level label is "Classroom Organization and Management" (matching ETS II.B.1.a). The earlier "Classroom Management and Academic Engagement" framing is rejected — engagement belongs to ACA-04.

#### Vocabulary

- **Classroom management** — Preventative behavior support; the upstream engineering of physical, temporal, and procedural environment that prevents most behavior problems.
- **Tier 1 behavior support** — Universal classroom-management practices applied to all students; foundation of MTSS behavior framework.
- **Physical environment** — Room layout, desk arrangement, traffic patterns, and visual organization that signal expected behavior.
- **Time management** — Routinizing transitions, lesson pacing, and instructional time use.
- **Transition** — Movement between activities; a frequent failure point in poorly-managed rooms.
- **Rules** — Stable, few (3–5), positively stated, posted, and reviewed expectations that define classroom behavior.
- **Routines** — Micro-sequences for recurring procedures (entering, turning in work, lining up); distinct from rules.
- **Positive reinforcement** — Specific praise or other contingent reward following desired behavior; primary lever in classroom management.
- **Specific praise** — Praise that names the behavior reinforced (e.g., "I like how you put away your folder before the timer ended"), more effective than generic praise.
- **4:1 reinforcement ratio** — Evidence-based target of four positive interactions for every one redirection.
- **Opportunity to respond (OTR)** — Frequency of student responses elicited per minute; high OTR rates correlate with engagement and reduced disruption.
- **Class-wide reinforcement system** — Group contingencies that reinforce collective compliance (e.g., Good Behavior Game, marble jar).
- **Co-construction of rules** — Collaborative creation of classroom expectations with students; correlates with stronger adherence than top-down rules.
- **Visual schedule** — Posted sequence of the day's activities used to communicate predictability and reduce transition anxiety.
- **Cue / signal** — A specific auditory or visual marker (timer, song, hand signal) used to initiate a routine.
- **Direct instruction zone** — The portion of the room where the teacher delivers whole-group instruction; should be free of cross-traffic.
- **High-traffic area** — Pencil sharpener, turn-in bin, bathroom pass — should be positioned away from instructional zones.
- **Evertson & Emmer** — Foundational classroom-management researchers whose work on transition recovery (15–20 minutes/day) is canon.
- **Classroom ecology observation** — Structured observation of the universal classroom environment; precedes individual behavior plans when multiple referrals come from one room.
- **House rules** — Common Praxis-question term for posted, positively-stated classroom expectations.

#### Misconceptions

- Believing strict rules and quick consequences are the foundation of good management, when in fact positively-stated expectations plus frequent specific reinforcement are the evidence-based foundation.
- Believing classroom management problems mean individual students need behavior plans, when in fact multiple referrals from one classroom indicate a universal Tier 1 management issue requiring environmental change first.
- Believing rules should be teacher-written and enforced from day one, when in fact rules co-constructed with students and revisited after breaks show stronger adherence across the year.
- Believing punishment, retention, or removal are appropriate first responses to classroom rule violations, when in fact the Praxis answer set consistently points to clear positive expectations + reinforcement instead.
- Believing transitions are inevitable lost time, when in fact effective routinization recovers 15–20 minutes of instruction per day.

#### Case archetypes

- **High-referral teacher case (Mr. Santos archetype):** Second-year teacher sends 14 referrals in 6 weeks. School psychologist conducts Tier 1 ecology observation, finds physical environment + negative rules + low praise ratio. Collaborative consultation yields rule rewrite, transition routine, 4:1 ratio target — referrals collapse.
- **Negative-rules-chart case:** Posted rules read as four prohibitions ("No running, No shouting…"). Recommendation: rewrite as 3–5 positively-stated expectations, teach explicitly, reinforce.
- **Long-transition case:** Transitions take 7–10 minutes. Recommendation: explicit routine + cue + practice during first weeks; sub-2-minute target.
- **Multiple-referral-from-one-room case:** Six students from one classroom referred for behavior plans in Q1. Recommendation: classroom ecology observation FIRST, before any individual FBA/BIP work.

#### Laws / Frameworks

- **MTSS — Tier 1 behavior layer** — Classroom management is the universal foundation; individual interventions belong to Tier 2/3.
- **Evertson & Emmer Classroom Management Research** — Empirical foundation for transition-time recovery and rule-routine distinction.
- **PBIS (Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports)** — School-wide framework whose classroom-level practices align directly with this skill.
- **Good Behavior Game** — Evidence-based class-wide reinforcement intervention from the Tier 1 toolkit.
- **NASP Practice Model — Direct & Indirect Services** — Frames consultation with classroom teachers as core school psychology practice.
- **OTR / opportunity-to-respond research base** — Greenwood and colleagues; high OTR rates correlate with academic engagement.

## Domain I — Professional Practices

### DBD-11 — Performance-Based Assessment

**Source module:** `MOD-GAP-03-performance-based-assessment.md`
**Proposed metadata ID:** `NEW-1-PerformanceAssessment`

#### Vocabulary

- **Performance-based assessment (PBA)** — Assessment evidence drawn from what the student actually produces (work samples, portfolios, performance tasks) rather than standardized item responses.
- **Work sample** — A single piece of student-produced work scored against a rubric (writing sample, math problem set, science poster).
- **Portfolio** — Curated collection of work samples over time, often combining best-work and process pieces, sometimes with student reflection.
- **Performance task** — Structured task in which a student demonstrates a complex skill (deliver a speech, conduct an experiment, solve a multi-step problem).
- **Authentic assessment** — Synonym/cousin for PBA emphasizing real-world, curriculum-embedded tasks.
- **Curriculum-embedded assessment** — Assessment generated from the regular instructional curriculum rather than a separate test event.
- **Analytic rubric** — Multi-criterion scoring guide that breaks performance into traits with descriptors per level; required for defensible PBA.
- **Holistic rubric** — Single overall score per work sample; faster but less diagnostic than analytic.
- **Rater training and calibration** — Structured process to align scorers' judgments before applying a rubric to student work.
- **Inter-rater reliability** — Statistical agreement between raters; defensible PBA requires documentation (Cohen's kappa > .70 or ICC > .80).
- **Triangulation** — Combining multiple evidence sources (standardized + work samples + teacher/family report) to converge on a decision.
- **Curriculum-based measurement (CBM)** — Brief standardized probes from regular curriculum; sits on the boundary between PBA and norm-referenced.
- **Reliability vs. authenticity trade-off** — Norm-referenced tests are high-reliability, low-authenticity; portfolios are high-authenticity, lower-reliability without rubric structure.
- **Reading-response journal** — Common portfolio artifact for evaluating reading comprehension over time.
- **Graphic organizer** — Visual structure capturing student thinking; useful as scaffolded vs. unscaffolded performance evidence.
- **Curriculum of record** — The actual instructional content the student receives; PBA evaluates performance within it.
- **Clinically meaningful discrepancy** — Gap between standardized and performance-based evidence that warrants structured investigation.
- **CLD considerations in PBA** — Performance-based evidence captures classroom functioning in less-biased ways than standardized instruments for culturally and linguistically diverse students.

#### Misconceptions

- Believing portfolios are subjective and indefensible for IEP decisions, when in fact portfolios scored against a well-constructed analytic rubric with documented inter-rater reliability are admissible and valuable.
- Believing work samples are inferior to standardized tests, when in fact they answer different questions — neither is inferior, and triangulation across both produces the strongest decisions.
- Believing performance tasks are too time-consuming for RTI/MTSS, when in fact short well-designed PBA probes (5-minute written retells, 1-minute oral reading fluency + comprehension response) are routinely feasible.
- Believing PBA can substitute for standardized assessment in eligibility decisions, when in fact the strongest practice is *triangulation* — neither source alone is sufficient.
- Believing student-curated portfolios bias the evidence, when in fact student selection and reflection are evidence-based features that increase metacognitive value.

#### Case archetypes

- **Reevaluation triangulation case (Diego archetype):** Student's WIAT-4 score improves; reading portfolio reveals growth is real but scaffold-dependent. IEP team continues services, shifts goals to inference + independent strategy use, documents portfolio in evaluation report.
- **Classroom-vs-test discrepancy case:** WIAT-4 in low-average range but classroom writing samples show coherent grade-level work. Recommendation: collect work samples, score against rubric, treat discrepancy as clinically meaningful.
- **Portfolio admissibility debate case:** IEP team debates whether portfolio is admissible. Guidance: yes, when scored against a defensible analytic rubric with documented IRR and used to *supplement* rather than replace other evidence.
- **CLD evaluation case:** PBA provides authentic curriculum-embedded evidence less susceptible to standardized-test bias for culturally/linguistically diverse students.

#### Laws / Frameworks

- **IDEA — multiple sources of data requirement** — Eligibility determinations must use a variety of assessment tools and strategies; PBA is one of those sources.
- **NASP Practice Model — Data-Based Decision Making domain** — Requires multi-source assessment evidence including authentic and curriculum-based forms.
- **Standards for Educational and Psychological Testing (AERA/APA/NCME)** — Provide reliability/validity criteria that apply equally to PBA rubrics.
- **MTSS / RTI** — PBA generates the qualitative evidence that complements CBM progress monitoring across the tiers.
- **Cohen's kappa / Intraclass correlation coefficient** — Statistical thresholds for documenting defensible PBA inter-rater reliability.
- **Larry P. v. Riles** — Case-law precedent reinforcing the obligation to use multiple, less-biased assessment sources for CLD students; PBA is one practical mechanism.

### DBD-12 — Assessment of Low-Incidence Exceptionalities

**Source module:** `MOD-GAP-07-low-incidence-exceptionalities.md`
**Proposed metadata ID:** `NEW-1-LowIncidenceAssessment`

#### Vocabulary

- **Low-incidence exceptionalities** — IDEA disability categories that are statistically rare and typically require specialized assessment approaches.
- **Hearing Impairment (HI)** — IDEA category for students with hearing loss adversely affecting educational performance.
- **Deaf-Blindness** — IDEA category for students with combined hearing + vision losses.
- **Visual Impairment including Blindness (VI)** — IDEA category requiring functional-vision assessment by a TVI.
- **Orthopedic Impairment (OI)** — Severe physical disability affecting school functioning.
- **Other Health Impairment (OHI)** — Chronic or acute health condition affecting alertness/strength/vitality.
- **Multiple Disabilities (MD)** — Concomitant impairments requiring coordinated multidisciplinary services.
- **Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)** — Acquired brain injury with separate IDEA eligibility status.
- **Standard battery limits** — Most cognitive/achievement tests were normed without low-incidence populations; norms generally do not apply.
- **Norms-based invalidity** — Risk that ad-hoc test adaptation invalidates norms without providing a defensible alternative.
- **Construct validity preservation** — Goal of choosing instruments and accommodations that still measure the intended construct.
- **Leiter-3** — Nonverbal cognitive instrument designed for use with hearing impairments, language disorders, or communication differences.
- **UNIT-2 (Universal Nonverbal Intelligence Test)** — Language-reduced cognitive instrument.
- **TONI-4 (Test of Nonverbal Intelligence)** — Nonverbal intelligence measure.
- **WISC-V with certified ASL interpreter** — One accepted approach for deaf students fluent in ASL when paired with trained interpretation.
- **Vineland-3** — Adaptive behavior scale critical when cognitive measures cannot capture functional skills.
- **ABAS-3** — Adaptive Behavior Assessment System; alternative adaptive measure.
- **Functional vision assessment** — Conducted by Teacher of the Visually Impaired (TVI), not the school psychologist.
- **Functional hearing assessment** — Conducted by educational audiologist.
- **Teacher of the Deaf/Hard of Hearing (TOD)** — Specialty role; partners with school psychologist on assessment.
- **Teacher of the Visually Impaired (TVI)** — Specialty role for visual impairment assessment and instruction.
- **Orientation and Mobility (O&M) specialist** — Trains students with visual impairments in safe travel.
- **Educational audiologist** — Specialist in school-based hearing assessment and amplification consultation.
- **Expanded Core Curriculum (ECC)** — Specialized curriculum for students who are blind/visually impaired (compensatory access, social interaction, recreation, self-determination, etc.).
- **Braille literacy assessment** — Specialized assessment for braille reading proficiency.
- **Certified interpreter for testing** — Interpreter trained in standardized test administration; required for defensible interpreted assessment.
- **Disclosure-of-interpreter-use** — Required practice in test reports when an interpreter is involved.
- **Multidisciplinary team integration** — School psychologist's role in low-incidence cases is to integrate findings, not solo-assess.

#### Misconceptions

- Believing a standard cognitive test can be used with any student if you extend time, when in fact extended time does not address sensory or motor access barriers — entirely different instruments may be required.
- Believing an untrained family member can interpret during testing, when in fact this introduces unknown error and compromises norms; certified interpreters trained in standardized administration are required, with disclosure in the report.
- Believing low-incidence students are too rare to require knowledge in this area, when in fact ETS explicitly tests this competency and a single misadministered evaluation has high stakes for placement and programming.
- Believing the school psychologist should administer all assessments alone, when in fact low-incidence assessment is inherently multidisciplinary; the psychologist integrates rather than solo-assesses.
- Believing a Full-Scale IQ can be reported when only a nonverbal composite is validly obtained, when in fact reporting a Full-Scale IQ that includes invalidly measured verbal subtests is indefensible.

#### Case archetypes

- **Profoundly-deaf-ASL-user case (Jaya archetype):** 8-year-old uses ASL as primary language. School psychologist convenes ASL interpreter, TOD, educational audiologist, family. Uses Leiter-3 for cognitive, Vineland-3 for adaptive, signed language assessment by TOD. Reports nonverbal IQ; does NOT report a Full-Scale IQ.
- **Multiple-disabilities case:** Student with cerebral palsy + severe hearing impairment. Defensible stance: convene multidisciplinary team (TOD, audiologist, OT/PT, SLP) and integrate adaptive + language + carefully-chosen cognitive instrument.
- **Untrained-interpreter case:** Bilingual staff member proposed as interpreter for testing. Best practice: decline; engage certified interpreter trained in standardized administration; document use and limitations.
- **Visual-impairment case:** Student is blind. Functional vision assessment by TVI; cognitive instruments must use tactile/verbal access; ECC evaluation alongside academic assessment.

#### Laws / Frameworks

- **IDEA disability categories — low-incidence cluster** — HI, Deaf-Blindness, VI, OI, OHI, MD, TBI; each with its own eligibility criteria.
- **Standards for Educational and Psychological Testing (AERA/APA/NCME)** — Require validity preservation under any accommodation or modification.
- **NASP Practice Model — Equitable Practices for Diverse Student Populations** — Encompasses low-incidence as well as CLD students.
- **Larry P. v. Riles** — General principle of using less-biased, multi-source assessment applies in low-incidence cases as well.
- **State certification standards for educational interpreters** — Many states require RID or EIPA certification for school-based ASL interpreters.
- **Expanded Core Curriculum (ECC) framework for blind/VI students** — Endorsed by APH and federal vision-impairment practice guidelines.

## Domain IV — Foundations

### RES-04 — Implementation Science and Change Management

**Source module:** `MOD-GAP-06-implementation-science.md`
**Proposed metadata ID:** `NEW-4-ImplementationScience`
**Note:** Full label is "Implementation Science and Change Management" per source module; matches ETS IV.B.4 wording.

#### Vocabulary

- **Implementation science** — Study of how evidence-based interventions get translated into real-world practice.
- **Implementation quality** — Third variable (alongside evidence base) that predicts student outcomes; without it, even strong interventions fail.
- **Fixsen's implementation stages** — Exploration → Installation → Initial Implementation → Full Implementation; each takes months to years.
- **Exploration stage** — Identifying need, selecting an EBI, assessing fit with context.
- **Installation stage** — Preparing infrastructure — training, policy adjustments, materials, coaching structure.
- **Initial Implementation stage** — Early use; fidelity fragile; support needs highest.
- **Full Implementation stage** — Routine scaled use with fidelity integrated into regular operations; typically reached at 2–4 years.
- **Stage-skipping** — Most common failure mode; jumping from exploration to initial implementation without installation.
- **Implementation drivers (Fixsen)** — Competency, Organization, Leadership drivers sustain any intervention.
- **Competency drivers** — Staff selection, training, coaching, performance assessment.
- **Organization drivers** — Decision-support data systems, facilitative administration, systems intervention.
- **Leadership drivers** — Technical leadership (right problem) + Adaptive leadership (change management).
- **Treatment fidelity / Treatment integrity** — Degree to which an intervention is delivered as designed.
- **Adherence** — Were the intended components delivered? (Dimension of fidelity.)
- **Exposure / Dosage** — How much? For how long? (Dimension of fidelity.)
- **Quality of delivery** — How well were components delivered? (Dimension of fidelity.)
- **Student responsiveness** — Did students engage? (Dimension of fidelity.)
- **Program differentiation** — Was this intervention distinct from others running concurrently? (Dimension of fidelity.)
- **Active ingredients** — Essential components of an intervention that produce the mechanisms of change; fidelity focuses here.
- **Peripheral components** — Formatting, pacing, local examples; can be adapted without threatening fidelity.
- **Fidelity checklist** — Developer-authored instrument; should NOT be improvised by implementers.
- **Barriers-and-facilitators assessment** — Done *before* initial implementation to identify resource, competency, cultural, technical, and leadership obstacles.
- **Sustainability** — Maintaining effective interventions at full implementation across time, staff turnover, and leadership change.
- **Evidence-based intervention (EBI)** — Intervention with documented efficacy in rigorous studies.
- **What Works Clearinghouse / Evidence for ESSA** — Evidence registries commonly used for EBI selection.
- **NIRN (National Implementation Research Network)** — Publisher of the Fixsen implementation frameworks.
- **Implementation audit** — Structured review of stages, drivers, and fidelity to diagnose why an EBI rollout is not producing expected outcomes.

#### Misconceptions

- Believing selecting an evidence-based intervention is the hard part, when in fact evidence registries make selection relatively straightforward — most failures occur post-selection in implementation.
- Believing an intervention that shows no effects after one year is not working, when in fact 2–4 years of implementation is typically required before full-implementation effects; an implementation audit is essential before abandoning an EBI.
- Believing fidelity is about following a script, when in fact it is about preserving the *active ingredients* that drive the mechanism of change; thoughtful adaptation of peripheral components is acceptable.
- Believing training alone is sufficient for implementation, when in fact training without coaching, leadership support, and data systems predictably fails — all three drivers must be present.
- Believing null outcomes can be interpreted without fidelity data, when in fact fidelity data is required to distinguish between "the intervention doesn't work" and "the intervention wasn't delivered."

#### Case archetypes

- **District-wide EBI rollout failure case (Riverside/Dr. Lin archetype):** Assistant superintendent declares Second Step "doesn't work" after one year with no fidelity data. Dr. Lin conducts implementation audit, finds skipped exploration/installation + absent coaching + 40% delivery rate. Recommends relaunch after revisiting installation with pilot + coaches + fidelity checklist.
- **Premature-evaluation case:** EBI rolled out; leadership wants outcome evaluation at 12 months. Recommendation: implementation audit first; Fixsen timeline suggests full implementation effects appear at 2–4 years.
- **Fidelity-interpretation case:** RCT-supported intervention yields null result. Without fidelity data, result is uninterpretable; with fidelity data, clarifies whether intervention is truly ineffective or poorly implemented.
- **Exploration-phase case:** District preparing to adopt a new behavior-management program. Most appropriate activity: fit assessment — staff buy-in, resource availability, competing programs, anticipated barriers — before commitment.

#### Laws / Frameworks

- **Fixsen's Implementation Stages (4 stages)** — Exploration, Installation, Initial Implementation, Full Implementation.
- **Implementation Drivers framework (3 drivers)** — Competency, Organization, Leadership.
- **Treatment Fidelity — 5 dimensions** — Adherence, Exposure/Dosage, Quality, Responsiveness, Program Differentiation.
- **NASP Practice Model — Research and Program Evaluation domain** — Encompasses implementation-science competencies.
- **NIRN Active Implementation Frameworks** — National Implementation Research Network publication of the active implementation model.
- **ESSA tiered evidence levels** — Strong, Moderate, Promising, Demonstrates a Rationale; applies to intervention selection in the exploration stage.
- **[VERIFY:] 2–4 year timeframe to full implementation** — Consistent with published NIRN guidance but flagged for citation verification in C2.

## Domain III — Systems-Level Services

### SSV-09 — School Climate Measurement and Evaluation

**Source module:** `MOD-GAP-09-school-climate-measurement.md`
**Proposed metadata ID:** `NEW-3-SchoolClimateMeasurement`
**Note:** Full label is "School Climate Measurement and Evaluation" per source module; aligned with ETS III.B.5 wording.

#### Vocabulary

- **School climate** — The felt quality of a school: safety, respect, connectedness, fairness; measured construct, not vague concept.
- **Triangulation in climate measurement** — Combining survey + behavioral/archival + observational data sources for a defensible climate picture.
- **Engagement (ED dimension 1)** — Relationships, respect for diversity, participation; one of three core climate dimensions.
- **Safety (ED dimension 2)** — Physical safety, emotional safety, bullying, substance use.
- **Environment (ED dimension 3)** — Physical condition, academic environment, discipline fairness, wellness.
- **ED School Climate Surveys (EDSCLS)** — Free, federally-validated survey suite (student / staff / family versions) covering all three dimensions.
- **Panorama Education School Climate Survey** — Widely-used commercial platform with benchmarks and subgroup disaggregation.
- **California Healthy Kids Survey (CHKS)** — Statewide implementation model used widely in research.
- **Georgia / Delaware School Climate Surveys** — State-specific validated tools.
- **Chronic absenteeism** — Missing ≥ 10% of enrolled school days; ESSA non-academic indicator and leading climate signal.
- **Office discipline referrals (ODRs)** — Behavioral indicator; disaggregation by race/disability/grade reveals climate gaps.
- **Suspension/expulsion disproportionality** — Climate signal when disaggregated rates differ markedly by subgroup.
- **Bullying report rates** — Reflect both prevalence and student comfort reporting; not interchangeable measures.
- **Teacher turnover** — Staff-side climate indicator.
- **School-Wide Evaluation Tool (SET)** — Observational instrument for PBIS fidelity.
- **Walkthrough protocol** — Structured observational climate audit.
- **Subgroup disaggregation** — Breaking down climate metrics by race, gender, disability, LGBTQ+ identity, EL status, SES.
- **Cell-size suppression** — Privacy-protection rule (typically n < 10 cells suppressed) that allows transparent reporting without violating individual privacy.
- **Climate-action cycle** — Measure → share with stakeholders → develop action plan → implement → re-measure (typically over 2–3 years).
- **Tier 1 climate interventions** — PBIS, SEL programs, restorative practices, trauma-informed systems changes.
- **ESSA 5th indicator / non-academic indicator** — Required school quality / student success metric in state accountability plans; chronic absenteeism is the most common.
- **Climate-improvement timeline** — 2–3 years to stabilize survey effects from Tier 1 interventions.

#### Misconceptions

- Believing a single climate survey constitutes the climate assessment, when in fact defensible work triangulates survey + behavioral/archival + observational data.
- Believing climate data should be confidential to protect the school, when in fact undistributed data cannot drive action; transparent stakeholder reporting is required, with privacy protected via aggregation/cell-size rules.
- Believing climate improvement should be visible within a year, when in fact Tier 1 climate-intervention effects typically take 2–3 years to stabilize; year-one effects must be judged against implementation fidelity.
- Believing school-wide averages capture climate, when in fact subgroup disaggregation is required — a 75% safety average can mask 50% subgroup scores.
- Believing low bullying-report rates mean low bullying, when in fact reporting rates also reflect student comfort and trust in the reporting system.

#### Case archetypes

- **Chronic-absenteeism trigger case (Central Middle / Dr. Patel archetype):** ESSA flags rising chronic absenteeism. Triangulated assessment (EDSCLS survey, archival ODR/attendance/suspension review, SET walkthrough) reveals unstructured-time engagement collapse + ODR disproportionality + family-side concerns. Three action priorities: unstructured-time redesign, Tier 1 SEL refresh with fidelity, family engagement plan. Re-measure at 12 months.
- **Survey-only assessment case:** School reports 78% "feel safe" overall score. Most appropriate next step: disaggregate by subgroup before concluding climate is healthy.
- **ESSA non-academic indicator case:** State must include non-academic indicator. Chronic absenteeism is the standard answer — both ESSA-eligible and validated as climate signal.
- **Year-one-evaluation case:** Climate intervention shows weak year-one effects. Defensible response: assess implementation fidelity before judging outcomes; full effects expected at 2–3 years.

#### Laws / Frameworks

- **ESSA non-academic indicator requirement** — Each state's accountability plan must include ≥ 1 non-academic school-quality indicator.
- **ED School Climate Surveys (EDSCLS) framework** — Three-dimension model (engagement, safety, environment); free federal instrument suite.
- **PBIS framework + SET fidelity assessment** — Universal Tier 1 climate framework with measurable fidelity dimensions.
- **Restorative practices framework** — Tier 1 alternative to exclusionary discipline; common climate intervention.
- **NASP Practice Model — Schoolwide Practices to Promote Learning + Services to Promote Safe and Supportive Schools** — Frame climate work across two NASP domains.
- **Civil Rights Data Collection (CRDC)** — Federal data collection requiring discipline disaggregation; supports climate-disparity surveillance.

### LEG-05 — ESSA (Every Student Succeeds Act)

**Parent skill:** LEG-02 (IDEA and Special Education Law)
**Source module:** `MOD-GAP-04-ESSA.md`
**Treatment:** Vocabulary + 2–3 misconceptions added to LEG-02's existing metadata; no separate progressTaxonomy entry. When K3b wires skillIdMap, LEG-05 shares LEG-02's metadata ID with additive content.

#### Vocabulary

- **ESSA (Every Student Succeeds Act)** — Federal K-12 accountability and funding law enacted December 2015; replaces NCLB in full by 2017–2018 school year.
- **ESEA (Elementary and Secondary Education Act)** — 1965 foundational law that ESSA reauthorizes.
- **NCLB (No Child Left Behind, 2001)** — Predecessor statute with federally-prescribed sanctions and 2014 universal-proficiency deadline; superseded by ESSA.
- **State-designed accountability systems** — ESSA's structural shift — states submit plans to USDOE; federal role is approval/oversight, not prescription.
- **Non-academic indicator (5th indicator)** — ESSA-required school-quality/student-success metric that is not academic achievement (e.g., chronic absenteeism, climate, engagement, advanced-coursework access).
- **Title I funds** — Federal funding for schools serving low-income students; ESSA requires EBIs when used for CSI/TSI interventions.
- **Comprehensive Support and Improvement (CSI)** — ESSA designation for lowest-performing schools; requires EBI with state oversight.
- **Targeted Support and Improvement (TSI)** — ESSA designation for schools with underperforming subgroups.
- **ESSA Evidence Tier 1 — Strong** — ≥1 well-designed, well-implemented experimental (RCT) study showing significant effect.
- **ESSA Evidence Tier 2 — Moderate** — ≥1 well-designed, well-implemented quasi-experimental study.
- **ESSA Evidence Tier 3 — Promising** — ≥1 well-designed correlational study with statistical controls.
- **ESSA Evidence Tier 4 — Demonstrates a rationale** — Logic model based on research + concurrent evaluation plan.
- **What Works Clearinghouse (WWC)** — Federal evidence registry that rates studies against ESSA tiers.
- **Evidence for ESSA** — Johns Hopkins–based registry rating programs on the ESSA tier ladder.
- **Annual assessment requirement** — ESSA retains reading/math testing in grades 3–8 + once in high school.

#### Misconceptions

- Believing ESSA is NCLB under a new name, when in fact the federalism structure is fundamentally different — ESSA devolves plan design and intervention strategy to states and eliminated NCLB's 2014 universal-proficiency deadline.
- Believing only Tier 1 (Strong) evidence is acceptable under ESSA, when in fact all four tiers are permissible depending on funding stream; Tier 4 provides a legal backstop for innovation with a required evaluation plan.
- Believing ESSA replaces IDEA for students with disabilities, when in fact ESSA (accountability/funding) and IDEA (individual entitlement/FAPE) are separate laws that coexist; Section 504 also applies simultaneously where relevant.
- Believing ESSA required a single federally-prescribed intervention for all low-performing schools, when in fact states design their own CSI/TSI intervention strategies within the evidence-tier framework.

### LEG-06 — Seclusion, Restraint, and Manifestation Determination

**Parent skill:** LEG-02 (IDEA and Special Education Law)
**Source module:** `MOD-GAP-05-seclusion-restraint.md`
**Treatment:** Sub-concept under LEG-02; expands LEG-02's metadata with additional vocabulary and misconceptions targeting ETS IV.C.2.c.

#### Vocabulary

- **Seclusion** — Involuntary confinement of a student alone in a room or area the student is prevented from leaving.
- **Restraint** — Use of physical, mechanical, or chemical means to restrict a student's movement.
- **Imminent danger of serious physical harm** — Federal-guidance threshold for any seclusion/restraint use.
- **Less restrictive alternatives** — Required to be attempted/considered before any seclusion/restraint use.
- **Prone restraint** — Face-down restraint position; prohibited in many states due to suffocation risk.
- **Mechanical restraint** — Use of devices (e.g., straps); generally prohibited or tightly regulated in schools.
- **Chemical restraint** — Use of medication to restrict movement; not authorized for school staff.
- **24-hour parent notification** — Common state requirement following any seclusion/restraint incident.
- **Manifestation determination (MD)** — IDEA procedural safeguard (34 CFR §300.530(e)) triggered by disciplinary change of placement >10 days.
- **10-school-day threshold** — Cumulative or consecutive removals beyond which a change of placement (and MD) is triggered.
- **Two-prong manifestation test** — (1) Was conduct caused by / directly related to the disability? (2) Was conduct the direct result of failure to implement the IEP?
- **Manifestation = causation, not severity** — Foundational test distinction.
- **Interim Alternative Educational Setting (IAES)** — Special-circumstances placement (up to 45 school days) for weapons, drugs, or serious bodily injury — applies regardless of MD outcome.
- **Special circumstances exception** — Weapons, drugs, serious bodily injury — allow up to 45-school-day IAES.
- **FAPE continuation during removal** — Required during any removal >10 school days regardless of MD outcome.
- **Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA)** — Required if not already completed when MD finds the behavior IS a manifestation.
- **Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP)** — Required to be implemented or reviewed/modified following a manifestation finding.
- **Pattern of removals** — Multiple shorter suspensions that cumulatively exceed 10 school days and constitute a change of placement.

#### Misconceptions

- Believing seclusion can be used when a student refuses to follow directions, when in fact federal guidance and most state laws limit seclusion/restraint to imminent danger of serious physical harm — never for discipline, convenience, or noncompliance.
- Believing manifestation determination is a judgment about behavior severity, when in fact it is a causation question — egregious behavior can be a manifestation; mild behavior can fail to be one.
- Believing a manifestation finding means no consequence for the student, when in fact the student cannot be disciplined the same way as a non-disabled peer but is still subject to disability-consistent consequences (BIP-driven response, therapeutic supports).
- Believing the IAES special-circumstances exception requires a manifestation finding, when in fact the exception applies regardless of MD outcome for weapons, drugs, or serious bodily injury.
- Believing FAPE pauses during disciplinary removal, when in fact FAPE must continue during any removal >10 school days, typically in an IAES.

### DBD-13 — Assessment Technology and Data Tools

**Parent skill:** DBD-01 (RIOT Framework and Multi-Method Information Gathering)
**Source module:** `MOD-GAP-08-assessment-technology.md`
**Treatment:** Sub-concept under DBD-01; expands DBD-01 metadata with platform-categories vocabulary and FERPA/decision-rule misconceptions targeting ETS I.A.2.i.

#### Vocabulary

- **Assessment technology** — Digital tools that collect, store, analyze, and visualize student data; modern infrastructure for data-based decision making.
- **Computerized CBM platforms** — aimsweb Plus, FastBridge (formerly FAST), STAR Assessments (Renaissance), DIBELS Data System, easyCBM.
- **Behavioral / SEL screening platforms** — BASC-3 BESS, SRSS-IE, SAEBRS, Panorama SEL Survey, Devereux Student Strengths Assessment (DESSA).
- **RTI / MTSS management systems** — Branching Minds, Panorama Student Success, Illuminate, PowerSchool Unified Insights; aggregate decision-support dashboards.
- **Electronic assessment administration** — Q-global / Q-interactive (Pearson, iPad-based WISC-V / WIAT), WJIV Online Scoring (Riverside), Conners-4 online.
- **Student Information System (SIS)** — Core district record system (PowerSchool, Infinite Campus, Skyward) — source of attendance, grades, demographic, and discipline data.
- **Trend line** — Best-fit line through progress-monitoring data points showing rate of improvement.
- **Aim line** — Line connecting baseline to goal on a progress-monitoring graph.
- **4-point decision rule** — Four consecutive data points below the aim line warrants an intervention change.
- **Risk flag** — Platform-generated alert that a student is at elevated risk; signals need for follow-up, not diagnosis.
- **Cut-score** — Threshold below which a student is flagged at risk on a screener; varies by platform/norm sample.
- **National norms** — Standardized comparison sample built into platform reports.
- **Data-sharing agreement** — Required FERPA-compliant contract with vendor specifying ownership, storage, breach notification, and destruction of student data.
- **Role-based access** — Tiered permissions (teacher / case manager / administrator) aligning with minimum-necessary principle.
- **Minimum necessary access** — Data security principle: each role sees only data needed for their function.
- **FERPA risk in free tools** — Free or trial tools that ingest student-identifiable data without a formal agreement violate FERPA at the institutional level.
- **Layered data view** — Combining screening + attendance + behavior + EL status + intervention history for differentiated tier placement.
- **Interpretation-not-diagnosis** — Ethical principle that platform alerts inform professional judgment but do not replace it.

#### Misconceptions

- Believing the platform's risk flag is a diagnosis, when in fact screening platforms flag *risk* and warrant follow-up — treating flags as diagnoses creates overidentification and legal exposure.
- Believing free internet tools are safe to adopt for student data, when in fact free-to-teacher tools often lack the institutional FERPA agreement; "convenient" is not a legal standard.
- Believing more data is always better, when in fact data quality and clear decision rules matter more than quantity — a 6-indicator system with clear criteria outperforms a 40-indicator dashboard with no rules.
- Believing all CBM platforms produce comparable risk classifications, when in fact a student flagged by one platform may not be flagged by another; cut-scores and norms vary.
- Believing role-based access is optional convenience, when in fact minimum-necessary access is a data-security and ethics requirement, not a UX nicety.
